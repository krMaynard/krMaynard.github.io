The 22x Gap: Where Android Malware Actually Lands

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

Google reports that at the end of 2024 a phone in the Philippines was ~22 times likelier to carry a harmful app than one in Japan — while managed enterprise devices ran ~8 times cleaner than the general fleet.

Bottom line: Google's Android security Transparency Report tracks the "Potentially Harmful Application" (PHA) rate — the share of devices or installs flagged as malicious — and the striking pattern is not time but place and management. For the 12-month window ending 2024-12-31, Google reports a PHA rate of 0.218% of installs in the Philippines and 0.202% in India, versus 0.010% in Japan and 0.016% in Germany: a spread of more than 20x across the ten countries Google breaks out. Managed enterprise devices (0.012%) stayed far below the all-device rate (0.099%). The caveat that keeps this from being a tidy "Android is getting safer" story: the all-device rate has drifted sideways near 0.1% since 2019, not fallen, and the country panel Google publishes changes from year to year.

  • Data: android_metrics (installs_by_country, devices_with_pha, installs, devices_by_version), Transparency Report API, 12-month rolling windows 2018–2024 and quarterly cuts 2017-Q1–2024-Q4.

Geography dominates the risk

PHA rates are a fraction of 1 in the source data; below they are shown as percentages of installs for the rolling year ending 2024-12-31. Google publishes ten countries, and they split cleanly into high-exposure emerging markets and low-exposure mature ones.

Country PHA rate (installs)
Philippines (PH) 0.218%
India (IN) 0.202%
Russia (RU) 0.109%
Brazil (BR) 0.094%
Indonesia (ID) 0.085%
Turkey (TR) 0.034%
Mexico (MX) 0.032%
United States (US) 0.032%
Germany (DE) 0.016%
Japan (JP) 0.010%

The top five all sit at 6–22x the Japanese rate. India is the one country that stays elevated across every window I checked (0.122% in 2020, 0.212% in 2022, 0.202% in 2024). The ranking is not perfectly stable, though: Japan itself reported 0.091% at end-2020 — near India's level that year — before falling to a tenth of that by 2024, and Russia spiked to 0.226% in 2022. So the "developing markets carry more malware" reading holds for the latest snapshot but should not be treated as a fixed law.

Managed devices, and the trend that isn't

The most durable gap in the data is management, not geography. Enterprise-managed devices — locked down, curated app catalogs — report a PHA rate an order of magnitude below the general population in every year Google publishes. What is not in the data is a clean downward trend: the all-device rate has oscillated in a narrow band, and Google Play's install rate actually spiked in 2022–2023 before receding.

Year-end All devices Enterprise devices Google Play installs
2019 0.103% 0.005% 0.099%
2020 0.122% 0.004% 0.064%
2021 0.086% 0.005% 0.052%
2022 0.094% 0.003% 0.211%
2023 0.159% 0.007% 0.193%
2024 0.099% 0.012% 0.082%

Enterprise devices ran 8x cleaner than all devices at end-2024, and were 20–30x cleaner in 2019–2022. The Google Play install rate is not a monotonic success story: it more than tripled from 0.052% (2021) to 0.211% (2022) before falling back to 0.082%. Whatever drove that bump, "steady annual decline" is not what these numbers show.

Newer Android, lower risk

Google's per-version cut (a separate measurement of the share of devices on each Android version flagged with a PHA) points the same way as common sense: the newest release is the safest. For the quarter ending 2024-12-31, Android 15 reported 0.257%, versus 0.393% (13), 0.402% (14), 0.520% (11) and 0.543% (12). By PHA type, the same quarter's Google Play detections were dominated by Privilege Escalation (28%), Spyware (26%) and Riskware (21%), with Toll Fraud and Trojans making up most of the rest.

Caveats. Every figure is Google's own self-reported PHA rate, a fraction of 1 that must never be summed across countries, versions or categories — each is an independent rate. The four cuts use different denominators (devices vs. installs) and windows (12-month rolling vs. quarterly), so cross-cut comparisons are directional, not exact; the per-version rates in particular are measured differently from the all-device rate and run higher. Google publishes only ten countries and their membership shifts between years, so the geographic ranking reflects the panel Google chose to disclose, not a global census. Data vintage: latest window ends 2024-12-31.
