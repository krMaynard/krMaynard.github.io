Detected by Machine, Decided by Whom?

July 14, 2026
EU DSA VLOP Transparency Data

"% automated" is the headline everyone cites. But it usually means *detection* — the machine found it. Whether a machine *decided* to act is a different number, and the two diverge by 39 points.

Bottom line: Across the DSA Statements-of-Reasons database, **86% of moderation actions were detected by automated means — but only 47% were decided fully automatically.** (Both global figures lean heavily on Google Shopping, ~40% of the whole corpus; excluding it, the rest of the ecosystem runs ~54% fully automated and only ~10% not automated.) The gap between detection and decision is human review — or at least human-review labelling — inserted between the flag and the enforcement. And platforms split into two opposite architectures for that gap: end-to-end automation (TikTok decides 92% by machine, AliExpress 93%, most marketplaces ~100%) versus auto-detect, human-decide — where Meta detects 95% automatically but records 0% fully-automated decisions, classing essentially every enforcement as human-in-the-loop. The number that matters for due process is the one nobody quotes.

  • Data: dsa_tdb_metrics, cuts by_automated_detection and by_automated_decision, H2 2025. Transparency Report API. Part 3 of the own-initiative series (Part 1, Part 2).

Two different questions

The DSA taxonomy carries two automation flags on every Statement of Reasons, and they answer different questions:

  • automated_detection — was the content found by a machine? (Yes/No)
  • automated_decision — who made the call to act? Fully automated / Partially automated / Not automated.

Detection is about surveillance reach; decision is about accountability. A platform can scan everything with automated tooling (detection = Yes) and still route every enforcement past a human (decision = Not/Partially automated). The DSA cares about both — Article 15(1)(e) requires reporting the use of automated means and their error rates — but the "X% automated" statistic in press coverage almost always means detection, the more flattering and less accountability-relevant of the two.

Globally (H2 2025): automated detection = 86%. Automated decision: 47% fully automated, 15% partially, 38% not automated. Detection is near-universal; unattended machine decision-making is a minority — under half. A composition warning, though: Google Shopping alone is ~40% of the corpus and files 58% of its decisions as not-automated. Exclude it and the remaining ecosystem is roughly 54% fully / ~36% partially / ~10% not automated — global percentages in a corpus this lopsided are largely statements about one marketplace.

End-to-end automation — detection and decision both near-total: TikTok (97%/92%), AliExpress (97%/93%), Shopify (100%/100%), Google Maps, Temu, Trustpilot. A machine finds it and a machine acts, at scale, with no human in the loop. TikTok is the standout among social platforms: 156 million fully-automated enforcement decisions in six months.

Auto-detect, human-decide — the mirror image: Meta (Facebook 95% detected / 0% fully-automated decision; Instagram identical) classifies ~95% of its decisions as partially automated. At Meta's volume (~a million enforcements a day) that cannot mean a person reviews each item — it is better read as a conservative labelling convention: automated action under human-designed oversight counted as "partially automated." Amazon (88% / 16% fully-auto, 84% partial) sits in the same camp. Which is precisely the point: "fully" vs "partially" automated is self-defined, and Meta and TikTok have chosen opposite labels for what are probably similar pipelines. It is a categorical split in disclosure — operations may or may not differ — and it's invisible if you only read the detection number, where Meta and TikTok look nearly identical (95% vs 97%).

A third group leans on human decisions outright — Twitch (100% not automated), Snapchat (81%), Kleinanzeigen (89%), Discord (74%), even Google Shopping (58% not automated across 2.2 bn actions — nominally ~1.3 bn human decisions on product delistings, i.e. ~7 m a day, a figure that again says more about labelling than staffing).

Why it matters

The accountability stakes attach to decision, not detection. A fully-automated decision means content was restricted with no human judgement — the case where error rates, appeal rights, and Article 22-style safeguards bite hardest. So the honest "how automated is this platform?" answer is the fully-automated-decision column, not the detection column the platform prefers to headline — with the caveat that it measures disclosed automation. On that measure, Meta and TikTok are opposite ends of the disclosure spectrum despite near-identical detection rates.

Caveats. Both flags are self-classified, and the definitions of "fully" vs "partially" automated are not audited — Meta's 0% could be a conservative labelling convention as much as a workflow fact. And the automation flags are internally inconsistent for some filers: Pinterest records ~1% automated detection and ~1% automated decision in its SoRs, flatly contradicting the 99% "solely automated" figure in its own Table 6 report (the same Pinterest inconsistency flagged in Parts 1–2).
