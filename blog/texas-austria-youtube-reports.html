Two Laws, One Global Spreadsheet: What Texas and Austria Get From YouTube

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

Google's Texas HB 20 filing reports 20.0 million YouTube video removals in the first half of 2025 — but only about 10% trace to US uploads. Austria's KoPl-G complaint channel, meanwhile, logged six reports in two years.

Bottom line: Two very different local transparency laws extracted very different things from the same platform. Texas's HB 20 (Bus. & Com. Code §120.053) gets a rich biannual filing — 20.0 million removals in 2025-H1, 96.9% first detected by automation, appeals up 51% period-over-period — but the numbers are YouTube's global Community Guidelines ledger, not Texas activity: the filing's own country breakdown attributes more removals to videos uploaded from India (25.4%) than from the entire United States (10.0%). Austria's KoPl-G, which mandated a dedicated user-complaint channel, recorded 6 reported items and 1 removal across its four semiannual reports (2021-H2 to 2023-H1). Everything here is Google's own self-reported data.

  • Data: regional_metrics, Transparency Report API, Texas (HB 20) 2024-H2–2025-H2; Austria (KoPl-G) 2021-H2–2023-H1.

The Texas filing is YouTube's global ledger, notarized in Austin

HB 20 requires a biannual transparency report from large platforms. What Google files is, on the evidence of the report itself, YouTube's worldwide Community Guidelines enforcement data. The removals-by-detection and removals-by-reason breakdowns each sum exactly to the headline totals (18,573,169 in 2024-H2; 20,025,866 in 2025-H1) — one consistent global dataset. And the removals-by-upload-country section gives the game away: the US accounts for 1.78M removals in 2024-H2 (9.6%) and 2.00M in 2025-H1 (10.0%), while India leads both periods at 5.1M (27.7%, then 25.4%). A Texas-only reading of these numbers would be off by an order of magnitude or more.

The filing also thinned out. Through 2025-H1 it carried eight sections (enforcement, appeals, flag sources, coordinated influence operations — 40 rows per period); the 2025-H2 filing contains exactly two metrics: demonetizations and age restrictions. Whatever the reason, the data window into the enforcement machinery closed after two periods.

Appeals surged; the win rate didn't move

Texas HB 20 filing (global CG data) 2024-H2 2025-H1
Videos removed 18,573,169 20,025,866
Removals first detected by automation 96.7% 96.9%
Appeals filed 851,851 1,289,571
Appeals as share of removals 4.6% 6.4%
Videos reinstated 118,673 175,862
Reinstated, as share of appeals 13.9% 13.6%
Reinstated, as share of all removals 0.64% 0.88%

Removals grew 7.8% between the two periods; appeals grew 51.4%. Yet the share of appeals that succeeded barely moved (13.9% → 13.6%) — roughly one reinstatement per seven appeals in both periods. Child Safety is the dominant removal reason in both filings: 55.1% and 57.4% of all removals, respectively.

169 million user flags, 41 government flags, 2 government removals

The filing's flag-source section pairs interestingly with its detection breakdown (which removals were first surfaced by each flagger type):

Flagger type, 2025-H1 Flags received Removals first detected Removals per 1,000 flags
Users 169,660,653 518,928 3.1
Organizations 200,208 93,428 466.7
Governments 41 2 —

Users generate a firehose of flags that converts at about 3 removals per 1,000; organizational flaggers convert at roughly 150 times that rate. Government involvement, at least as measured here, is nearly nonexistent: 41 flags and 2 first-detection removals in six months, on a platform removing 20 million videos. Meanwhile the two metrics that survive into 2025-H2 move in opposite directions: demonetizations fell 46% year-over-year (4.62M in 2024-H2 → 2.48M in 2025-H2) while age restrictions rose 50% (3.77M → 5.64M).

Austria built a mandated complaint channel; six people used it

Austria's KoPl-G §4 reports cover the channel the law itself required. Across four semiannual reports — 2021-H2 (0 reported, 0 removed), 2022-H1 (5, 0), 2022-H2 (1, 1), 2023-H1 (0, 0) — the totals are 6 reported items and 1 removal. Users evidently kept using YouTube's native flagging (169M+ flags per half-year globally) rather than the statutory webform. The Austrian series ends in 2023-H1, before the EU's DSA superseded national regimes: a two-year natural experiment in which a bespoke national reporting channel went essentially unused.

Caveats. All figures are Google's self-reported filings, unaudited. The Texas enforcement numbers are explicitly global Community Guidelines figures, not Texas-specific activity; the country breakdown lists only the largest upload countries (covering 51–56% of removals), so per-country shares are of the global total, not of a complete list. Flags received and removals-by-first-detection are different counting frames — a low conversion rate does not mean flags were wrong, since a flagged video may already have been actioned. Latest vintage: Texas 2025-H2 (reduced scope), Austria 2023-H1.
