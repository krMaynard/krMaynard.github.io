How France Quietly Became LinkedIn's Biggest Government Requester

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

LinkedIn logged just 13,379 member-data requests in a decade — and in the latest half-year, France alone accounted for nearly half of them.

Bottom line: LinkedIn's government-requests report describes a footprint a fraction the size of any consumer platform's — about 13,379 member-data requests worldwide from 2016 through 2025, plus a mere 756 government content-removal requests. But the composition has quietly flipped: France, which filed a handful of requests a year through 2020, surged past the United States in 2024 and issued 874 requests in the second half of 2025 — 49% of the global total — versus 443 from the US. The caveat is that these are LinkedIn's self-reported counts of requests received, not a measure of what was ultimately handed over, and the report offers no explanation for the French spike.

  • Data: linkedin_metrics (member_data_requests, content_removal_requests), Transparency Report API, 2016-H1 through 2025-H2.

A rounding error next to the consumer platforms

Across the full run of LinkedIn's reporting, governments sent roughly 13,379 member-data requests — the kind of number a Meta or Google clears in a matter of days. Volume has climbed steadily, from 145 requests in the first half of 2016 to 1,778 in the second half of 2025, a 12-fold rise. Yet even that growth leaves LinkedIn an outlier: a professional network holds résumé histories and connection graphs, not the private messaging and location trails that drive law-enforcement demand elsewhere.

Disclosure has not kept pace with volume. Computing a global rate as accounts-disclosed over accounts-subject, LinkedIn reports handing over data on somewhere between 37% and 74% of targeted accounts in any given half-year, with no upward trend — the two most recent periods sit at 49% and 37%. Ten years and a 12x rise in requests later, LinkedIn still discloses on roughly half of the accounts named, no more reliably than it did in 2016.

France overtakes the United States

For most of the report's history this was an American story: the US filed 6,415 member-data requests cumulatively, more than double second-place France's 3,058. But almost all of France's total is recent. France sat in the single digits per half-year through 2020, then accelerated sharply.

France crossed the US in the first half of 2024 and has pulled steadily ahead since, while American volume flattened in the low 400s. Germany, a distant third at 164 requests in 2025-H2, is the only other country in double-digit percentages. The report tells us the requests arrived; it does not tell us why French authorities suddenly found a professional network so interesting.

Who asks a CV site to remove content

Government content-removal requests — demands that LinkedIn take material down rather than hand over data — are a separate, far smaller stream: 756 requests total since 2018, of which LinkedIn reports acting on 613 (81%). The country map here looks nothing like the data-request one. Turkey leads, and China — where LinkedIn wound down its localized service in 2021 — ranks third.

The countries that dominate data requests (France, Germany) barely register for removals; the countries that dominate removals (Turkey, China, Indonesia) barely appear in the data-request tables. Whether a government leans on LinkedIn to identify a member or to erase content appears to track the government, not the platform.

Caveats. Every figure here is LinkedIn's own count of requests received; disclosure and action figures are also self-reported, and none of it audits whether the requests were lawful or the compliance appropriate. Counts are exact, but the labeling is imperfect — LinkedIn tagged its US rows "US" in 2019 and "United States" everywhere else, which must be combined to get the true American total. The report gives no context for the French surge, and country totals mix long-reporting states with newcomers, so cumulative rankings understate how recent the French shift is. Latest data: 2025-H2 (report generated May 2026).
