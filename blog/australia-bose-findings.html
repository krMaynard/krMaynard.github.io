What Platforms Admit Only When a Regulator Asks

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

Compelled by Australia's eSafety Commissioner, WhatsApp disclosed a median 27-hour wait before a human moderator acted on a child-sexual-abuse report — and two AI-companion apps disclosed employing zero trust and safety staff.

Bottom line: Australia's Basic Online Safety Expectations notices force out numbers voluntary transparency reports never contain — median human-response times per service, trust-and-safety headcounts, user-report volumes at the individual-service grain — and they show a 50-fold spread in how fast platforms act on child sexual exploitation and abuse (CSEA) reports: a median 33 minutes on Threads versus 1,621 minutes (about 27 hours) on WhatsApp, across 11.46 million global user reports in six months. The biggest caveat sits inside the compelled data itself: these are the companies' own answers to legal notices, for a single 15 June–15 December 2024 window, and some medians rest on almost no reports.

  • Data: esafety_bose_metrics, Transparency Report API, periods 2024-06-15..2024-12-15 (CSEA periodic), 2025-07-01..2025-09-30 (AI companions), 2026 (children's survey).

A number no voluntary report publishes: how long a report waits

DSA-style transparency gives you aggregate removal counts. eSafety's notices, issued under the Online Safety Act 2021 in July 2024, instead compelled 17 services to state their global CSEA user-report counts for six months — and the median time a human moderator took to reach an outcome.

WhatsApp Channels 8,656 980 16 h

Facebook Messenger 30,700 499 8 h

Microsoft Teams 778 101 <2 h
Snapchat 1,361,148 90 1.5 h
Discord 86,990 58 <1 h
Threads 46,900 33 33 min

Google Meet reports the period's worst median — 5,952 minutes, over four days — but received exactly one report, so it says little. WhatsApp's 27-hour median, by contrast, sits on 811,208 reports; Snapchat shows a human outcome in a median 90 minutes while handling 1.36 million.

Composition matters: Meta's four services account for 8,877,600 of the 11,458,969 global reports — 77 per cent. Ex-Meta, the total is about 2.58 million, over half of it Snapchat. Australia-specific figures exist for only three scopes: 374,261 reports across all services, of which Meta reports 39,749 (0.45% of its global volume) and Snapchat 27,796 (2.0%).

The near-zero column

The compelled grain also exposes the other tail. Google Chat and Google Meet each report a single global user report of CSEA in six months; Google Messages reports zero; Outlook.com, 74. On services of this scale, counts that low are hard to read as an absence of harm — the more plausible reading, and the one eSafety has publicly pressed, is that they measure how hard it is for users to find and use a reporting tool. A voluntary report would simply omit the metric; a notice makes the near-zero visible.

AI companions: 1,527 CSEA reports, and two firms with no safety staff

Character.AI 1,527 5,333 37 5.0

*Pornography + CSEA + self-harm categories; Chub AI reported only the CSEA category. Chai's 6.5 is a fractional FTE figure.

Character.AI reports 1,527 CSEA user reports in a single quarter — about 41 per trust-and-safety staffer — on a service that 5 per cent of Australian children aged 10–17 say they have ever used (eSafety's 2026 representative survey; 8 per cent report ever using any AI companion). Chub AI and Nomi report zero trust and safety staff while logging CSEA reports. Headcount disclosures like these exist nowhere in voluntary reporting.

Caveats. Everything here is what companies reported in response to legal notices, not audited behaviour: "Facebook reports 6,700,000" is the accurate phrasing, and Meta's figures are rounded (the per-service rows sum to 7 more than the stated 11,458,969 total). The CSEA stream covers one six-month window (to December 2024, published August 2025, updated February 2026), so no trend can be drawn. Medians are non-additive and, where volumes are tiny (Google Meet, n=1), meaningless. Meta dominates the global report total; Australia-level counts are published for only three scopes, so the 374,261 national total cannot be decomposed by service. Report counts conflate user behaviour, product reach and reporting-tool design — a low count is not evidence of a safe service.
