The Warrant Took Over: 13 Years of Police Data Requests at Naver and Kakao

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

In 2012, Naver and Kakao report handing user identities to Korean authorities roughly 26,800 times without a warrant. Since 2013 that number is zero, and annual seizure-warrant requests have grown almost tenfold, to 34,499 in 2025.

Bottom line: Korea's two dominant platforms report that voluntary disclosure of subscriber identities ("communications user information" under the Telecommunications Business Act) stopped abruptly after 2012, both companies have processed essentially none since 2013-H1, while court-issued seizure warrants became the workhorse of law-enforcement access, rising from 3,661 combined requests in 2012 to 34,499 in 2025, with Kakao absorbing about three-quarters of the load. The biggest caveat: these are the companies' own half-yearly reports, and one warrant can sweep in enormous numbers of accounts, so account totals are lumpy and much larger than request counts suggest.

  • Data: korea_metrics, Transparency Report API, half-yearly periods 2012-H1 through 2025-H2.

The 2012 switch-off

In 2012 alone, Naver reports processing 16,549 warrantless user-information requests covering 68,184 accounts; Kakao (Daum + Kakao services) reports 10,229 processed requests covering 68,330 accounts, over 136,000 account identities handed over in a single year. From 2013-H1 onward, both companies report processing zero such requests in every one of the 26 half-year periods (Kakao's Daum shows a single processed request in 2013-H1, then nothing). Authorities never fully stopped asking: Kakao still received 1,660 user-information requests in 2025 and reports complying with none of them. Requests to Naver dwindled to zero by 2025-H2.

Warrants filled the gap, mostly at Kakao

As voluntary disclosure ended, seizure warrants surged, and the two platforms diverged sharply. Naver's warrant volume jumped early (142 requests in 2012-H1, 4,283 by 2013-H1) and then plateaued around 3,500–5,000 per half. Kakao's kept climbing for a decade, peaking at 19,418 requests in 2024-H1 before easing to 12,211 in 2025-H2, still 15 times its 2012-H1 volume.

*Naver's rate is its own reported figure; Kakao's is computed as processed ÷ requests.

Naver's reported compliance rate on warrants has drifted steadily down, from 88% in 2012-H1 to 70% in 2025-H2. The account counts behind Kakao's warrants are staggering: from 625,270 accounts in 2015-H1 (the first period its messenger arm reports the figure) to a peak of 5,715,759 in 2024-H1 and 4,028,387 in 2025-H2, several hundred accounts per processed warrant, consistent with warrants that capture whole group chats or broad user sets.

The other two request types are fading, at different speeds

Communications confirmation data (metadata such as logs, delivered without a full warrant until stricter rules attached) tells a migration story. Naver's volume fell 85%, from 3,894 requests in 2012-H1 to 572 in 2025-H2, while Kakao's stayed flat (2,218 then, 3,107 now), so a category once concentrated on the portal now lands overwhelmingly on the messenger. Compliance differs too: Naver reports processing 87% of these requests in 2025-H2, while Kakao's processed-to-request ratio is about 65%. Communication-restricting measures (interception-type orders) have nearly vanished: 58 combined requests in 2012-H1, a low of 2 in 2020-H2, and 18 in 2025-H2, though both companies process nearly all of the few they receive (Naver reports 100% in every period).

Caveats. All figures are self-reported by Naver and Kakao under their half-yearly transparency programs; latest vintage is 2025-H2. Kakao's platform totals combine its Daum and Kakao service corporations, and its messenger arm did not report seizure-warrant account counts before 2015-H1, so early Kakao account totals are understated. Account counts are extremely lumpy (Naver reports 7.1 million accounts under seizure warrants in 2017-H1 alone against ~3,400 processed warrants), so trends in accounts are far noisier than trends in requests. Naver did not report user-information request counts for 2012, only processed totals; and its 2020-H2 seizure-warrant request count (1,034) is inconsistent with its processed count and reported rate, so that cell was excluded. Zero processed requests means the companies report no compliance, it does not mean authorities cannot obtain the data by warrant.
