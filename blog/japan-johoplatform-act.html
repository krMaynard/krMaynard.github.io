Japan's new platform law: three companies, three different answers

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

The first filings under the 情プラ法 are in. Meta logged 1.5 million rights requests, YouTube logged 2,450, and LY Corp logged none at all — because each read the law differently.

Bottom line: Japan's Information Distribution Platform Act (情プラ法, in force April 2025) requires MIC-designated large providers to publish annual implementation statistics, but the first filings show the three providers disclosing at wildly different grains: LY Corporation reports only bulk post and removal counts for its five services, Google/YouTube maps the law onto its existing global legal-and-policy transparency report, and Meta is the only one that actually built the law's rights-request channel — complete with response-time buckets. And Meta's headline number is deceptive: of the ~1.5 million rights requests it received, roughly 99% were copyright and trademark takedowns, not the defamation and privacy complaints the law was written to address.

  • Data: japan_metrics, Transparency Report API — LY Corp FY2024 (Apr 2024–Mar 2025), YouTube and Meta from designation (late Jul 2025) to Mar 2026.

Each filer answered a different question

The law is new and there is no standard template, so each provider mapped it onto whatever it already reported. The three disclosures barely overlap:

Provider Grain disclosed Formal request volume Response-time data Reason categories
LY Corp (5 services) Posts, removals, removal-rate none no no
Google (YouTube) Legal + policy streams 2,450 legal requests no yes
Meta (FB/IG/Threads) Rights-request channel + enforcement 1,504,057 requests yes (≤7d / >7d) yes

Only Meta reports the mechanism the 情プラ法 is really about — a formal channel where anyone can demand removal of content that violates their rights, with the provider obliged to decide and notify promptly. YouTube instead reports its standard "legal removals" stream (2,450 requests, of which just 289 items, or 7.1%, were removed) and its community-guidelines enforcement (162,390 videos removed, 7.7 million user flags). LY Corp reports neither requests nor response times at all — just how many posts existed and how many it took down.

LY Corp's five services: volume without context

LY Corporation is the Japanese incumbent, and its filing is the thinnest. It gives one table: total posts, posts removed, and a removal rate, for each of its five services. The removal rates are strikingly consistent and low:

Service (LY Corp, FY2024) Posts Removed Removal rate
LINE OpenChat 5,514,828,787 6,980,935 0.1%
LINE VOOM 403,331,897 3,055,002 0.8%
Yahoo! News comments 113,995,832 1,277,396 1.1%
Yahoo! Chiebukuro 66,199,309 444,727 0.7%
Yahoo! Finance boards 29,415,652 487,773 1.7%

LINE OpenChat is the giant here — 5.5 billion posts, more than ten times every other service combined — yet it removes the smallest share, 0.1%. The highest removal rate belongs to Yahoo! Finance boards (1.7%), the stock-message forum, where spam and market manipulation are perennial. But because LY Corp discloses no request volumes, no category split, and no response times, there is no way to know how much of this was user-requested versus proactive, or how fast anything was handled.

Meta was fast — but mostly about copyright

Meta's channel is the only one that lets you judge the law's core promise: timely response. And on speed, the numbers are emphatic. Of the 649,169 Facebook requests Meta decided to act on, 648,391 — 99.9% — were resolved within seven days; only 778 took longer. But the composition undercuts the headline. Facebook received 1,344,304 rights requests, and 1,338,662 of them (99.6%) fell under "Trademarks, copyrights, and neighboring rights." The complaints the 情プラ法 was actually motivated by — honor, portrait rights, invasion of privacy — numbered just 5,642 on Facebook, under half a percent.

Meta rights channel Requests Actioned ≤7d Actioned >7d No action Copyright/TM share
Facebook 1,344,304 648,391 778 695,135 99.6%
Instagram 156,048 37,243 1,065 117,740 96.3%
Threads 3,705 1,037 23 2,645 70%

Across all three services Meta actioned only a minority of requests — 48% on Facebook, 25% on Instagram — with the rest recorded as "no action taken." Threads, the newest and smallest service (20.6 million monthly users in Japan), is the one place where honor-and-privacy complaints rival copyright, but its volumes are trivial.

Caveats. All figures are self-reported and the reporting windows do not align: LY Corp covers Japanese FY2024 (before the law bit), while Meta and YouTube cover roughly August 2025 to March 2026. Metrics are not comparable across providers — LY Corp counts posts, YouTube counts flags and legal items, Meta counts rights requests and actions; percentages and Meta's rounded "approx" figures must not be summed. Meta's per-category splits are its own illustrative "most prevalent" subset, so a section's "Total" is not the sum of the listed categories. Meta's rights totals tie out exactly (received = actioned ≤7d + actioned >7d + no action). Nothing here ranks the providers; it shows one legal mandate produced three incompatible reports.

Data: japan_metrics, Transparency Report API — LY Corp FY2024 (2024-04..2025-03); YouTube 2025-07-26..2026-03-31; Meta 2025-07-30..2026-03-31.
