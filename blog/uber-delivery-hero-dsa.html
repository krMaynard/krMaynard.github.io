Uber Is Paying $14.8 Billion for 60 Million Users Regulators Have Never Counted

July 16, 2026
Uber Delivery Hero EU DSA Transparency

Uber agreed this week to buy Delivery Hero for $14.8 billion, €41.50 a share, all stock, operations in 50 markets. The headline metric in the coverage: Uber gains access to roughly 60 million monthly active users, mostly in markets where it barely operates today.

That number is the only one we've got.

I run a query API over platform transparency reports, EU DSA filings, government removal requests, the works. The DSA makes platforms publish their EU user counts (average monthly active recipients, or AMAR) precisely so regulators don't have to take investor-deck numbers on faith. My current snapshot covers the second half of 2025, with user-count filings from 90 services and content-moderation decisions from 60 platforms.

Delivery Hero appears in that data exactly zero times. Not under its own name, not as Glovo, not as foodora, not as efood.

It's not that they're hiding, it's better than that. Delivery Hero maintains a dutiful DSA statement page, updated this February, covering precisely the same six-month window as my snapshot (July–December 2025). It names all four EU brands, foodora, Glovo, efood, foody, and for each one discloses the same thing: the user count is "estimated to be well below the threshold" for designation as a very large online platform. Four brands, four estimates, zero digits. The 45-million-user VLOP line is the only number on the page, and it belongs to the regulation, not the company.

The page does link two DSA transparency reports, which is where the real figures should live. The 2025 report link returns a 404, and the file behind it has never been captured by the Internet Archive, so as far as the public record is concerned it may as well not exist. The transparency report is missing from the transparency page.

The 2024 report is the one thing you can actually get, and I got it: a ZIP of CSVs from the pre-harmonization era, before the Commission's mandatory reporting template kicked in for periods starting July 2025. Here is a year of content governance across Delivery Hero's four EU platforms, in full. Orders from member-state authorities: zero, everywhere. Notices of illegal content: 4,419 for Glovo, 42 for efood, 25 for foodora, 9 for foody, about 4,500 in twelve months, against the 4.65 million notices the major platforms in my dataset logged in six. No user counts anywhere in the ZIP, and the "date of publication" field for three of the four brands still reads YYYY-MM-DD. Nobody, not the company, apparently not anyone, is reading these files closely.

Which makes the 404 sting more: the 2025 report, the missing one, would be Delivery Hero's first filed under the EU's now-mandatory harmonized template. The first report in the binding, machine-readable, comparable format is the one you can't download.

The lone food-delivery company that does show up in my dataset is Delivery Hero's rival Just Eat Takeaway, which submitted a DSA transparency report for 2025 covering moderation governance and automated means.

So the asset at the center of a $14.8 billion deal is measured entirely by the seller. Sixty million monthly active users, $42 billion in gross bookings, and not one figure filed under a regime built to verify exactly this kind of claim, the number for investors, the shrug for regulators. Food delivery is one of transparency reporting's dead zones: big enough to reshape Uber's global footprint, small enough per brand to stay invisible.

Uber's shareholders will get diligence. The rest of us get a press release.
