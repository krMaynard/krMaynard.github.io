Child Safety Runs Discord's Enforcement Machine

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

In the first half of 2024, Discord reports disabling 357,056 accounts, and just under half of them, 178,165, were for one policy category: Child Safety.

Bottom line: Discord's own transparency data shows child protection has become the center of gravity of its Trust & Safety operation. Child Safety accounts for 49.9% of all accounts disabled in 2024-H1, dwarfing every other policy, and Discord's referrals to the U.S. National Center for Missing & Exploited Children (NCMEC) grew roughly 5x, from 10,695 in 2022-Q1 to 55,955 in 2023-Q4. The biggest caveat: these are self-reported figures, the reporting grain switches from quarterly to half-yearly in 2024 (so 2024-H1 must be halved to compare), and one early quarter (2022-Q2) simply duplicates 2022-Q1 in the source data.

  • Data: discord_metrics, Transparency Report API, 2022-Q1 through 2024-H1 (quarterly 2022–2023, half-yearly from 2024).

Accounts and servers get actioned for different reasons

Discord punishes individual accounts and whole servers on the same menu of policies, but the mix is strikingly different. Accounts are overwhelmingly disabled for Child Safety. Servers, which host communities rather than people, are removed most often for Regulated or Illegal Activities, the catch-all covering drugs, weapons, hacking, and financial fraud, where Child Safety ranks only second.

Policy category Accounts disabled Servers removed
Child Safety 178,165 7,462
Exploitative & Unsolicited Content 48,571 1,835
Regulated or Illegal Activities 26,810 15,107
Deceptive Practices 24,322 1,207
Violent & Graphic Content 20,657 3,163
Harassment & Bullying 19,283 1,210
All categories (total) 357,056 41,002

Child Safety is 49.9% of account disables but only 18% of server removals; Regulated or Illegal Activities is 36.8% of server removals but 7.5% of account disables. In other words, Discord reports banning people mostly to remove predators, and shutting down servers mostly to remove illicit marketplaces and communities.

The NCMEC surge

The clearest trend in the dataset is the growth in reports Discord files with NCMEC, the legally mandated clearinghouse for online child sexual abuse material in the United States. Almost all of these are "media reports", flagged images or videos, with a much smaller stream of "grooming or endangerment escalations."

Period Media reports Grooming/endangerment Total to NCMEC

2024-H1* 103,035 609 103,644

<small>*Half-year figure; ≈51,800 per quarter, in line with 2023-Q4. 2022-Q2 is omitted because the source duplicates 2022-Q1.</small>

Quarter over quarter, total reports rose about 5.2x between early 2022 and late 2023, and grooming escalations grew even faster, roughly 6x, from 54 to 317. Once the 2024 half-year is put on a quarterly footing (~51,800), the pace looks flat versus 2023-Q4 rather than continuing to climb. Rising numbers here are ambiguous: they can reflect more abuse on the platform, better detection tooling, or both, and Discord's reports do not let you separate the two.

The requests profile: overwhelmingly American

For a global chat platform, Discord's government data-request load is heavily domestic. In 2024-H1 it reports 3,782 U.S. government information requests (producing information in 3,223), against just 545 international requests spread across 40 countries. Brazil (106), Germany (90), and the United Kingdom (64) lead the international column, but no single foreign government comes close to U.S. law-enforcement volume, American requests outnumber all others combined by about seven to one.

Caveats. Every figure here is self-reported by Discord and reflects its own policy taxonomy and detection systems, not an independent audit. The reporting period changes from quarterly (2022–2023) to half-yearly (2024), so 2024-H1 totals must be halved before comparing to a 2023 quarter; the apparent jumps in NCMEC reports and U.S. requests into 2024 largely dissolve once that grain change is accounted for. The 2022-Q2 NCMEC breakdown is an exact copy of 2022-Q1 in the underlying data and was excluded. Percentages such as appeal-grant rates are stored as reported rates and were never summed. "Accounts disabled" counts enforcement actions, not distinct users, and the very large 2022 account-disable totals (over 1 million in 2022-Q1) reflect bulk spam removals that later fell sharply.

Data: discord_metrics, Transparency Report API, 2022-Q1 to 2024-H1.
