Two Readings of “Own Initiative” on DSA Table 6 — and the One Every VLOP Chose

July 13, 2026
EU DSA VLOP Transparency Data

The DSA's Table 6 asks platforms to report content moderation done on their “own initiative” — a term open to a narrow and a broad reading. Across the first harmonised reports, every VLOP took the broad one.

Bottom line: "Own initiative" on Table 6 can be read two ways. On the narrow reading, a platform reports only the content it proactively — automatically — detected and actioned itself. On the broad reading, it reports all the content moderation it initiated, human- and machine-detected alike, that wasn't compelled by a Member-State order or an Article 16 notice. The two readings can differ by orders of magnitude. Across the first harmonised reporting period (H2 2025), every VLOP took the broad reading: the headline own-initiative figure is the full universe of provider-initiated Terms-&-Conditions enforcement, and proactive/automated detection appears only as a nested sub-count within it (0% to ~99% of the total, depending on the platform).

  • Source: t6_own_initiative_tos on the Transparency Report API (transparency.kieranmaynard.com), reporting period H2 2025 (2025-07-01 → 2025-12-31) — the first period under the harmonised template. 24 VLOP services / 18 parent platforms.
  • Legal frame: Commission Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/2835, Annex sheet 6_own_initiative_TC (Art. 15(1)(c) DSA).

1. What the template actually requires (the definitional answer)

Table 6 is not a single number per category. Each category row carries two distinct count columns, defined verbatim in the Implementing Regulation:

Col Field Regulation instruction (verbatim)
F Number of measures taken at the provider's own initiative "This field must indicate the total number of measures that have been taken at the provider's own initiative. Own initiative means that the decision to impose a restriction … has not been preceded by an order from a Member State authority or a notice submitted through a notice-and-action mechanism (Art. 16)."
G Number of measures taken after detection with solely automated means "This field must indicate the number of measures that were taken at the provider's own initiative solely using automated means."

The scope of Table 6 is therefore defined by exclusion (not order-driven, not notice-driven), not by detection method. "Own initiative" (Col F) and "proactive/automated detection" (Col G) are orthogonal:

  • Own initiative = who initiated the action (the platform itself, not an order or a user notice).
  • Proactive detection = how the content was found (solely automated means).

A platform can act on its own initiative via human review (counts in F, not G), and Col G is explicitly a subset of Col F — the API's own field definitions restate this: measures = "Count of own-initiative moderation actions"; automated = "Of those actions, the count taken by automated means."

This is the interpretive fork. Read broadly, Col F carries every own-initiative action (as its instruction — "the total number of measures" — states) and Col G merely discloses how much of it was automated. Read narrowly — treating "own initiative" as shorthand for "what we proactively caught" — a platform would report only the automated-detection figure. The Col F text points to the broad reading; whether platforms actually followed it is an empirical question, settled below.

Two facts show every VLOP took the broad reading

  1. Col F ≥ Col G in every single row. No platform reports the automated-detection count as its own-initiative total. Under the narrow reading (Table 6 = proactive detection only), F would equal G. It never does.
  1. The restriction breakdown reconciles to Col F, not to Col G (ratio ≈ 1.00 for 23 of 24 services). The 14 restriction-type columns are reported per action, independently of detection method. They sum to the own-initiative total (F) — proving F is the complete action universe. Under the narrow reading, the restriction columns (which cover all actions) would over-sum F. They don't.

Together these show every VLOP populated the headline metric with the whole of provider-initiated T&C moderation — the broad reading — with automated detection carved out as an internal subset.

The automated share varies enormously — and that's the point

Because Col G is a genuine sub-count, its share of Col F is a real behavioural signal, not an artefact of scoping:

  • Automation-dominant (≈90–99%): Pinterest, XNXX, AliExpress, X, Google Search/Maps/Play, YouTube — near-total automated detection, but F still exceeds G.
  • Mixed (~60–90%): TikTok, Meta, Temu, Snap, LinkedIn, App Store.
  • Human-detection-heavy (F ≫ G): Amazon 15.7%, Booking.com 7.3%, Zalando 22.3%, Pornhub 0.04%, SHEIN 0%. These platforms report large own-initiative totals of which little or none was solely automated detection — only possible because Col F counts human/manual-detected actions too. These are the clearest cases of the broad reading — under the narrow reading their Table 6 figures would collapse to a fraction of what they reported.

The same structure holds on the illegal-content sheet (t5): measures ≥ automated throughout (e.g. Google reports 2.7 bn own-initiative illegal-content measures; Shein again reports automated = 0).

3. Caveats & data-quality notes

  • "Measures" vs "statements of reasons." Table 6 counts measures/actions, which are the events that generate own-initiative T&C statements of reasons. They are the SoR-generating universe, but the two need not be 1:1 (a single SoR can bundle multiple restrictions; some actions on account-level or manifestly illegal content may not each produce a distinct SoR). The finding — F = the full own-initiative set — stands regardless.
  • Bing is the one reconciliation outlier (restriction-sum ÷ measures = 0.92): its filing contains an entry where measures = 7,220 but the restriction columns sum to only 152 (= its automated count). That is an internal inconsistency in Bing's own report, not a different scoping choice; Microsoft's other services (LinkedIn) reconcile cleanly.
  • Null vs zero. Google's "Multi-Services" line and several Google Search sub-surfaces leave Col G null (not reported) rather than 0; SHEIN and Google's t5 report an explicit 0. Treat "not reported" and "zero automated" distinctly.
  • Minor over/under-sums (TikTok 1.01, Snapchat 0.97, AliExpress ~1.003) are within ~1–3% and consistent with a single action carrying multiple restriction types — they do not change the conclusion.
  • Comparability. Figures are H2-2025 VLOP filings only (report_tier = vlop); non-VLOP filers in the same table report over different windows and were excluded.

4. External corroboration: the DSA Statements-of-Reasons database

The transparency reports are self-declared. The DSA Transparency Database (Statements of Reasons) is an independent source: every in-scope moderation decision is filed as an individual SoR, and each SoR carries source_type (incl. Own-initiative) and automated_detection (Yes/No) as separate fields. Using the Commission's dsa-tdb re-aggregation (dsa_tdb_metrics, same H2-2025 window), these marginal cuts test the report finding from outside.

Global (all platforms, H2 2025): the two populations are plainly different sizes —

  • source_type = Own-initiative: 3,633,572,041 SoRs
  • automated_detection = Yes: 3,150,399,935 · No: 527,615,482

Own-initiative exceeds automated detection by ~0.48 bn, and 528 m SoRs are non-automated. So in the raw filing data, "own initiative" is a strictly larger set than "proactive detection" — the same relationship the report's Col F ⊇ Col G encodes.

Per service — does Table 6's figure track own-initiative or automated detection? The report's F is compared against each service's SoR own-initiative count and SoR automated-detection count:

Two corroborating results:

  1. Magnitude. For clean matches, report F ≈ SoR own-initiative: Google Shopping 1.00×, Google Maps 0.98×, Amazon 1.03×, Instagram 1.05×, Facebook 0.93×, Pinterest 1.09×, Booking 1.18×. F reconciles to the own-initiative SoR universe — not to a proactive-detection subset of it.
  1. The discriminator. In the 5 services where the SoR own-initiative and automated-detection populations diverge by >20% (so the test can actually discriminate), F is closer to own-initiative in 5 of 5. The cleanest is Pinterest: F = 184 m matches SoR own-initiative 169 m, not SoR automated-detection 1.6 m. Had Pinterest scoped Table 6 to proactive detection, it would report ~1.6 m. It reports 184 m. Same verdict for SHEIN, Pornhub, XVideos, Google Play.

Caveats (why this is corroboration, not reconciliation):

  • The two systems count different things. Reports count measures/actions; the database counts statements of reasons. They are not expected to match exactly, and some diverge sharply: Temu (report 768 m vs 134 m SoRs), SHEIN (2.7 m vs 90 k), Snapchat (11.3 m vs 2.4 m), and X (20.9 m vs just 0.2 m SoRs — X files almost no SoRs). These are known report-vs-database gaps (counting unit, de-duplication, timing, under-filing) and do not bear on scoping — the scoping test only asks which SoR population F tracks, and even for these it tracks own-initiative.
  • Col G ≠ SoR automated_detection=Yes. Report Col G is solely automated means; the SoR flag is any automated means used to identify content (includes partially-automated). This explains why report automated-share sits below SoR automated-share for Amazon (16% vs 88%) and Booking (7% vs 95%). Pinterest is the reverse (report 99% "solely automated" vs SoR 0.9% automated-detection) — an internal inconsistency in Pinterest's own filing between the two systems, worth noting but immaterial to the F-scoping question.
  • These are marginal cuts (no source×automation×ground joint in the aggregation), so the test is on magnitudes and partitions, not exact cells.

The independent SoR data therefore corroborates the core finding: Table 6's own-initiative metric is the full own-initiative population, with proactive/automated detection a distinct, generally smaller sub-population.

5. The scope boundary: are user reports "own initiative"?

A prior question sits underneath the F/G finding: what counts as own-initiative in the first place? The answer is not clean, and it is the template's softest joint.

The exclusion is narrow. Column F excludes only two triggers — a Member-State order, or "a notice submitted through a notice and action mechanism as referenced in Article 16." And Article 16's mechanism is, by its own terms, for notifying content a user "considers to be illegal content" — not the channel for "this breaches your Community Standards." So user reports divide three ways:

  1. User reports illegal content via the notice-and-action mechanism → Article 16 notice → out of Table 6 (belongs in Table 4).
  2. User reports a ToS/Community-Standards violation not through the Article 16 illegal-content mechanism → not an Article 16 notice → not excluded by Column F's wording.
  3. Blended report flow → depends on how the platform classifies it.

For category 2 two textual arguments put such reports inside Table 6: (i) expressio unius — the definition lists only orders and Article 16 notices as exclusions, so anything else is not carved out; (ii) exhaustive taxonomy — Art. 15(1) routes all moderation into orders (Table 3), Article 16 notices (Table 4), or own-initiative (Tables 5–6), and a non-Article-16 ToS report fits neither of the first two. The counter-reading is channel-based (any notice via the report button is excluded) and matches the intuitive sense of "own initiative," but it isn't what the operational definition says. The Commission has not resolved this, and researchers have flagged it as disarranged harmonisation across reports.

Empirically, VLOPs have mostly resolved it inclusively — but classify wildly inconsistently. Using the SoR database's by_source_type cut per service (H2 2025):

Two readings of this:

  • The specific "non-Article-16 ToS report" gap is small for VLOPs. Across these services, other-notification is 0.03% of classified SoRs — most platforms fold ~everything (≥99%) into own-initiative, i.e. take the inclusive reading by default. So Table 6 for the big platforms already absorbs user-flagged ToS actions; the ambiguity rarely changes their headline number. The clean exception is Pornhub (16.3% other-notification), whose Table 6 measure tracks own-initiative + other-notification (F ≈ OI+ON, ratio 1.00) — a platform visibly taking the textual reading.
  • But the classification itself is wildly inconsistent, which is the real comparability problem. The same "did a user flag it?" question is answered three incompatible ways: most platforms label ~100% own-initiative; X labels 100% other-notification; Snapchat (72%) and XVideos (86%) route the majority through Article 16 notices. Identical underlying behaviour would land in Table 6 for one platform and Table 4 for another. The ambiguity doesn't vanish — it moves one layer up, into source-type classification, and undermines cross-platform comparability of the own-initiative vs notice split.

Caveats: source_type is self-classified by each platform; the near-total "own-initiative" for most VLOPs may partly be a labelling convention rather than genuinely unprompted detection. X files very few SoRs (0.2 m) relative to its Table 6 measures (20.9 m), so its 100%-notification profile is not representative. Marginal cuts only.

6. Conclusion

Wherever Table 6's "own initiative" could be read narrowly or broadly, every VLOP took the broad reading. On the detection axis (§1–2), the headline figure is the entire body of provider-initiated T&C enforcement — human- and machine-detected alike — not the proactive-detection subset; automated detection is disclosed only as a nested sub-count (Column G), 0%–99% of the total across platforms, and the restriction-type breakdown reconciling to the own-initiative total (not the automated sub-total) rules out the narrow reading. On the harder, genuinely-unsettled source axis (§5), most VLOPs likewise fold user-flagged T&C actions into own-initiative rather than treating them as notices.

But this is a convergence of practice, not of clear legal command. The Regulation compels the broad reading on detection method (Col F's "total number" instruction), yet leaves the source boundary open — and there platforms' classifications diverge wildly (§5). Read Table 6 accordingly: a broad own-initiative aggregate whose cross-platform comparability rests on interpretive choices the template does not fully pin down.
