What Platforms Police vs What Users Report

July 14, 2026
EU DSA VLOP Transparency Data

Own-initiative moderation is 4.3 billion actions. User notices are 4.7 million. And they are about almost entirely different things — platforms proactively police what machines catch cheaply, while the harms people actually flag stay reactive.

Bottom line: Split the DSA's two enforcement channels by category and they invert. Own-initiative moderation — 4.26 bn VLOP actions in H2 2025 — is dominated by generic terms-of-service violations (74%), intellectual property (7%), and unsafe products (7%); the contested-speech harms (hate, harassment, violence) together are ~3%. User notices — 4.65 m of them — are the mirror image: illegal/harmful speech is 34% and IP 26%. So the thing users overwhelmingly report (speech) is a rounding error in what platforms proactively act on, and vice versa. Proactive enforcement scales where automation is cheap and unambiguous; the hard, context-dependent harms are left to the reactive channel — 900× smaller. One caution up front: this inversion is substantially composition — the own-initiative base is marketplace volume (Google Shopping alone is 69% of the generic-ToS bucket; the top three marketplaces ~88%), while speech notices flow to social platforms. It is a structure-of-the-ecosystem finding, not a claim about each platform's behaviour.

Three things the split shows

1. At ecosystem level, proactive volume is not where the speech harms are. "Illegal or harmful speech" — hate, harassment, threats — is 0.7% of own-initiative actions but 33.8% of user notices. Much of that is composition (the own-initiative denominator is marketplace catalogue enforcement; speech notices concentrate on social platforms — a per-platform cut would look very different), so the defensible claim is narrower than "platforms wait to be told": proactive volume lives where automation is cheap, and no platform's proactive output is dominated by contested speech. That tracks Part 4's finding (Facebook's proactive rate for hate/violence was ~70%, vs 99% for spam) and extends it to the whole VLOP corpus: the categories where proactive detection is hard are exactly the ones that show up in the notice channel.

2. Proactive enforcement is a machine-friendly-category story. The billions of own-initiative actions concentrate in categories with cheap, unambiguous signals — generic ToS spam, IP (hash/fingerprint matching), unsafe products (catalogue scanning), scams. This is the same automation-economics point as Part 3, now at the category level: platforms proactively act where a classifier is confident, and 74% of it lands in a bucket labelled only "other."

3. "Other ToS violation" is a 3.1-billion-action black box — mostly a marketplace one. Nearly three-quarters of all own-initiative moderation is filed under the catch-all category with no further description, and 69% of that bucket is a single filer (Google Shopping; the top three marketplaces are ~88%). The single largest fact about proactive enforcement on VLOPs is that we don't know what it's for. That's the category-level echo of the whole series: the headline own-initiative number is big, universal — and, on inspection, uninformative.

Conclusion — and the series in one line

Across five parts, the DSA's own-initiative metric has been: broadly read but under-defined (Part 1), not comparable across platforms or self-consistent within one (Part 2), conflated detection with decision (Part 3), incomparable across regimes (Part 4), and — here — concentrated in machine-friendly categories and a 3-billion-action "other" bucket, while the harms users care about stay reactive — a split that is as much marketplace-vs-social composition as platform behaviour (Part 5).

The through-line: "own-initiative content moderation" reads as a single, impressive, multi-billion number, and almost every way you cut it, the number means less than it looks. Transparency data is only as useful as its definitions — and this metric's definitions don't hold their shape across platforms, regimes, or categories.
