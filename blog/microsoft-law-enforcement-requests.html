The Two-Country Club: Who Actually Gets Content From Microsoft

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

Across 427,023 criminal law-enforcement requests since 2017, Microsoft reports handing over customer content just 19,103 times, and 96% of those disclosures went to two countries: the United States and Brazil.

Bottom line: Microsoft's law-enforcement report shows that actual content, emails, documents, photos, almost never leaves the building: only 4.5% of criminal requests from 2017-H1 through 2025-H1 ended in a content disclosure, and outside the US (12.5% content rate) and Brazil (25.2%), the rest of the world combined got content on just 0.27% of its 309,061 requests. The big caveat is that this is Microsoft's self-reported accounting, and its report format changed in 2017, so the 8.5-year figures above are pinned to the "criminal" section only.

  • Data: microsoft_metrics, Transparency Report API, half-yearly 2013-H1 – 2025-H1 (criminal section 2017-H1 – 2025-H1; combined section 2013–2016).

Four outcomes, one rare one

Every criminal-process request in Microsoft's report lands in exactly one of four buckets, and in this dataset the buckets partition the total to the request: 19,103 content disclosures (4.5%), 249,613 non-content disclosures (58.5%), 63,135 "no data found" (14.8%), and 95,172 rejections (22.3%) across 427,023 requests from 2017-H1 to 2025-H1. In other words, when Microsoft complies, about 63% of the time, it overwhelmingly hands over subscriber and transactional metadata, not what customers actually wrote or stored.

Request volume itself is strikingly flat: every half-year since 2017 has landed between 21,433 and 28,593 criminal requests. What is not flat is scope per request, accounts specified hit 101,115 in 2025-H1, roughly double the ~50,000 typical of recent halves, and the US alone accounts for 63,279 of them (up from 31,018 in 2024-H1).

Content is a two-country phenomenon

The report labels the US inconsistently across vintages ("United States" in most halves, "USA" in 2023-H1 and 2025-H1); merging the two, the picture is stark. The US filed 90,281 criminal requests and received content 11,293 times, 59% of all content disclosures worldwide. Brazil, with a content rate of 25.2%, took most of the rest. Germany, the single largest requester, received content zero times in 8.5 years, as did the UK, France, and India.

This split matches Microsoft's long-standing public framing that it discloses content only under binding legal orders in the jurisdictions where it can be compelled, chiefly US warrants, so the near-zero rate elsewhere is a legal-scope artifact, not a measure of how aggressive each country's police are. Rejection rates, meanwhile, vary enormously: India sees 46% of its requests rejected and Italy 41%, versus 9% for the UK.

Rejections have climbed for a decade

The older "combined" section (2013–2016) lets the trend run back further, though it isn't directly comparable to the criminal-only era. Within each section, the direction is the same: Microsoft rejects a growing share of what it receives.

Period Section Requests Content rate Non-content rate Rejection rate
2013-H1 combined 37,196 2.2% 77.2% 2.4%
2016-H2 combined 25,837 3.7% 64.3% 15.5%
2017-H1 criminal 25,367 4.3% 63.0% 16.6%
2021-H1 criminal 27,809 4.4% 50.0% 27.9%
2025-H1 criminal 28,593 4.7% 59.3% 22.5%

The rejection rate went from 2.4% of requests in 2013-H1 to 15.5% by late 2016, then from 16.6% to a 2021-H1 peak of 27.9% in the criminal era, settling around 22–23% since. Non-content disclosure moved the opposite way, from 77% of requests in 2013 to the high-50s today.

Caveats. All figures are Microsoft's self-reported counts; latest period is 2025-H1. Numbers are pinned to one section at a time, "combined" (2013–2016) and "criminal" (2017 on) are different report regimes and shouldn't be summed, the 2013 "skype" section overlaps "combined", and the small "civil" section counts accounts rather than requests for its outcome metrics, so it is excluded here. Rates are computed from summed counts, not averaged percentages. The US appears under two labels ("United States"/"USA") and is merged above; aggregates are US-heavy for content (59% of all disclosures), so ex-US figures are given where it matters.

Data: microsoft_metrics, Transparency Report API, 2013-H1 – 2025-H1.
