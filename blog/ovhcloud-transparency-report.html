The DSA Report With No Takedowns: Inside OVHcloud's 2024 Numbers

July 16, 2026
Transparency Data

Europe's biggest cloud host received 3.3 million abuse reports in 2024 — but only 28% of them were even in scope for the Digital Services Act, and just five countries' authorities sent it any orders at all.

Bottom line: OVHcloud, the Roubaix-based hosting giant, files its DSA transparency report as an infrastructure intermediary — a host that cannot see or selectively remove what its customers store — so unlike a social platform it reports no content removals at all. What it does disclose is telling: of 3,296,689 reports it received in 2024, only 923,765 (28%) fell within DSA scope, the other 72% being infrastructure abuse (DDoS, malware, spam) that isn't "content" under the law. And the authority orders it received were extraordinarily concentrated — 6,919 orders in total, of which France and Belgium alone sent 90.6%. The biggest caveat is definitional: this is a hosting provider's-eye view, where "illegal content" means a customer's server, not a post, so none of these figures are comparable to a platform's moderation stats.

  • Data: ovhcloud_metrics, Transparency Report API; OVHcloud DSA transparency report, 17 Feb – 31 Dec 2024.

Five countries, and two of them sent almost everything

The DSA obliges intermediaries to report the orders they receive from member-state authorities to act against illegal content or to hand over information (Articles 9, 10 and 15). OVHcloud names just five origin countries for 2024, and the distribution is lopsided: France (3,339) and Belgium (2,932) together account for 6,271 of the 6,919 total orders — 90.6% — while Germany (293), Poland (306) and Spain (49) make up the rest. That France leads is unsurprising for a French company headquartered in France; Belgium's near-parity is the more striking line in the table.

OVHcloud says it acted on most orders with a median of 8 hours. The one outlier is Spain, where the median time to give effect to an order was 145 hours — roughly six days — against a handful (49) of orders, so a few slow cases move the median a long way.

What a host actually gets: mostly not "content"

Report volume tells the opposite story to the orders. Through its Article 16 abuse form OVHcloud logged 3.3 million reports in 2024, but it counts only 923,765 as DSA-relevant illegal-content notices; the remaining 2,372,924 (72%) concern activities — DDoS attacks, intrusions, spam, malware — that affect infrastructure rather than being unlawful content. Within the in-scope notices, one category swamps the rest: intellectual-property infringement at 794,377 notices, 86% of the total. Phishing (96,075) is a distant second, and the genuinely severe categories are numerically small — 2,415 child-sexual-abuse reports and 1,055 violent-or-shocking.

(The percentages are shares of the 923,765 DSA-scope total. A sixth category, data-protection complaints, drew 4,642 reports but is excluded from that total — data-protection breaches aren't DSA "illegal content," which is exactly why the six categories sum to 928,407 while the headline in-scope figure is 923,765.)

The automation column is where OVHcloud's infrastructure posture shows: the high-volume, low-severity streams are the most automated — 49.5% of phishing and 42.8% of IP reports are handled without a human — while abuse categories that demand judgment (CSAM 2.2%, violent 9.5%) are almost entirely manual. Median response times are measured in seconds, not the hours or days platforms report, because for a host "action" is triage and routing, not a moderation decision.

Caveats. Every figure is self-reported by OVHcloud, and this is a narrative PDF, not the EU harmonised template, so its categories are the company's own and don't map cleanly onto other filers'. The report period (17 Feb – 31 Dec 2024) is a partial first year and predates the H2 2025 window most platforms now report, so it isn't time-comparable. Number formatting in the source is inconsistent — France's orders print as "3 339" but Belgium's as "2932" — and both are reproduced exactly as published. Most important, this is a hosting report: "illegal content" here means a customer's site or server, and OVHcloud forwards content-specific notices to the customer rather than moderating, so none of this measures content moderation in the sense a social platform would.
