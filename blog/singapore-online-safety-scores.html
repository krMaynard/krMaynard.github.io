The Regulator That Mystery-Shops Big Tech

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

Singapore's IMDA filed real user reports on six platforms and timed the responses. Instagram acted on 2% of them in the first round, while telling the same regulator it catches 88–99.6% of violating content proactively.

Bottom line: IMDA's Online Safety Assessment is one of the few places a regulator tests platforms itself and publishes comparable scores, and the scores diverge sharply from the platforms' own filings: in the round covering Apr 2024–Mar 2025, local forum HardwareZone acted on 93% of IMDA's legitimate user reports while TikTok, the only service to get worse, acted on just 25%, even as TikTok's own report says 97% of its Singapore video removals were proactive. The caveat is scale: the benchmark is a mystery-shopper sample of user reports, so a low action rate means reports went unaddressed, not that the platform removes little content overall.

  • Data: singapore_metrics, Transparency Report API, assessment rounds 2023-08..2024-07 and 2024-04..2025-03; platform self-reports 2024-04..2025-03.

The regulator's own scoreboard

Under Singapore's Code of Practice for Online Safety, the six Designated Social Media Services file annual reports, but IMDA also runs its own benchmark, submitting legitimate user reports of harmful content and measuring what happens. Two rounds are in the data:

Service Action rate, 2023-08..2024-07 Action rate, 2024-04..2025-03 Time to action, 2023-08..2024-07 Time to action, 2024-04..2025-03
HardwareZone 89% 93% 3 days 2 days
Facebook 53% 81% 9 days 4 days
X 54% 74% 10 days 5 days
YouTube 46% 68% 5 days 4 days
Instagram 2% 54% 7 days 4 days
TikTok 39% 25% 5 days 4 days

Three things stand out. First, HardwareZone, a Singapore hardware forum, not a global platform, tops both rounds on both metrics. Second, being scored publicly appears to move behavior: five of six services improved their action rate between rounds, and every service got faster, with Facebook cutting its time to action from 9 days to 4 and X from 10 to 5. Instagram's move is the starkest: from an action rate of 2%, the number that made headlines when IMDA published its first assessment, to 54% a year later. Third, TikTok went the other way, falling from 39% to 25% to become the worst performer of the second round despite acting within 4 days when it acted at all.

What the platforms say about themselves

The self-reported sections for Apr 2024–Mar 2025 tell a much sunnier story, though each vendor reports its own metrics and none are directly comparable:

Service Self-reported Singapore figure (2024-04..2025-03) IMDA action rate, same period
Facebook 561,000 pieces actioned; proactive rate 83.1–98.8% by category 81%
Instagram 533,108 pieces actioned; proactive rate 87.7–99.6% by category 54%
TikTok 754,231 videos removed proactively vs 20,493 otherwise (~97% proactive) 25%
X median time to action 69 hours 74%
YouTube 445,908 flags received; 72,126 videos removed 68%

The two stories can both be true, because they measure different pipelines. Meta's proactive rates describe content its systems caught before anyone reported it; IMDA's benchmark tests the reactive pipeline, what happens when an ordinary user flags something. Notably, Facebook's lowest self-reported proactive rate is Bullying and Harassment at 83.1% (Instagram's second-lowest is the same category at 94.0%), which is exactly the kind of context-dependent harm where user reports matter most, and where the mystery-shopper found the biggest gaps. X reports a median time to action of 69 hours (~2.9 days) against IMDA's measured 5 days, though the samples and definitions differ.

Composition notes: YouTube's flag volume is dominated by "Spam or Misleading" at 233,115 of 445,908 flags (52%); its removal categories are a different taxonomy, so flags and removals don't divide cleanly. Meta's counts are rounded, category-level figures, and summing them assumes no cross-category overlap.

Caveats. The platform_report section is self-reported and vendor-defined, "Facebook reports 561,000 pieces actioned" is a claim in a filing, not an audited fact, and metric names are not comparable across services. IMDA's action rate and time to action come from a sampled mystery-shopper exercise whose report volumes per service aren't in this dataset, so small samples could move percentages. The two assessment rounds cover overlapping but different windows (Aug 2023–Jul 2024 vs Apr 2024–Mar 2025), so round-over-round changes partly reflect different periods. Latest data vintage: Apr 2024–Mar 2025.

Data: singapore_metrics, Transparency Report API, 2023-08..2024-07 and 2024-04..2025-03.
