The Moderator Gap: 33,000 People for the EU's Biggest Platforms

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

Across 19 of the EU's largest platforms, dedicated content moderators range from Temu's 28 per million users down to Wikipedia's 0.11 — and Irish and Maltese share just six moderators each, ecosystem-wide.

Bottom line: In their H2 2025 DSA transparency filings, 19 very large platforms report 33,097 people dedicated to content moderation — but four-fifths of them (26,276) are outsourced contractors, not employees. Staffing per user varies more than 250-fold: joining headcount to average monthly active recipients (AMAR) for the 15 services that report both, the median is 8.4 moderators per million EU users, led by Temu (27.8) and trailing at Wikipedia (0.11), whose model leans on volunteers. The steepest gap is linguistic: while English draws 23,014 moderator language-competencies, Irish and Maltese draw six apiece, from just three platforms each. Every figure is self-reported and unaudited, and the two tables cover overlapping-but-different sets of services, so the per-user ratios are indicative, not exact.

  • Data: t9_human_resources and t10_amar, Transparency Report API, VLOP/VLOSE filers, H2 2025 (2025-07-01/2025-12-31).

Moderators per million users: a 250-fold spread

Table 9 reports each platform's internal (employed) and external (contracted) moderators. Summed across 19 services, that is 33,097 people — 6,821 internal and 26,276 external, meaning 79% of the reported moderation workforce is outsourced. Dividing each platform's total by its AMAR from Table 10 gives a rough staffing intensity. Only 15 services appear in both tables (see caveats), so this is a partial picture:

The floor is instructive: Wikipedia reports 17 paid moderators against 155.7M monthly recipients because its moderation is overwhelmingly volunteer community work that a paid-headcount metric never captures. The adult-content sites (XVideos, XNXX at 1.22) and Booking.com sit near the bottom; social and marketplace platforms with heavy proactive obligations staff most densely.

The small-language coverage gap

Table 9's third indicator counts moderators with "sufficient linguistic expertise" per language. The scope codes are messy — one filer uses uppercase country-style codes (DE, CZ, DK) while most use lowercase ISO codes (de, cs, da) — so codes were canonicalized to the 24 official EU languages before summing. All 24 are covered by at least one platform, but coverage collapses at the small end:

English alone accounts for 23,014 of 46,861 EU-language competencies — 49% of the total. At the other end, Irish and Maltese — both official EU languages — are served by six moderators each, drawn from only three platforms. A further 6,630 moderators are reported as language-"Agnostic." The pattern is a familiar one: languages with the smallest speaker bases get the thinnest dedicated coverage, even as the DSA obliges platforms to moderate content in every member-state language.

What the headcount does not show

The 79%-outsourced split matters for interpretation: most of this workforce sits with third-party contractors, and headcount says nothing about hours, tooling, or the automation that handles the first pass at scale. A platform reporting few moderators is not necessarily under-moderating — it may be leaning on automated systems or, like Wikipedia, on unpaid volunteers. Headcount is one input to moderation capacity, not a measure of outcomes.

Caveats. All figures are platforms' own H2 2025 DSA filings, unaudited. The per-user table joins two tables that do not cover the same services: four high-headcount services (YouTube, Google Maps, Google Play, Google Shopping) report moderators but no AMAR here, while four high-AMAR services (Instagram, Facebook, Bing, Pornhub) report AMAR but no headcount — so the ratios cover only the 15-service overlap and understate the ecosystem. AMAR is an average-monthly-recipients figure; headcount is a point-in-time count; the ratio mixes the two. Language totals sum each platform's per-language counts, so a multilingual moderator is counted once per language (competencies, not distinct heads), and the case-canonicalization of scope codes is an interpretation. Total rows (scope_is_total) were pinned to avoid double-counting breakdown rows.
