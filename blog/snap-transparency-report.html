What Snapchat's Moderators Actually Remove

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

Sexual content and harassment account for two of every three enforcement actions Snap took in late 2024 — and the categories it treats most carefully are the ones it handles slowest.

Bottom line: In the second half of 2024, Snap reports 10.03 million Trust & Safety enforcement actions against 5.68 million unique accounts, and the mix is lopsided: Sexual Content (38.5%), Harassment and Bullying (27.1%), Drugs (11.1%) and Child Sexual Exploitation (9.6%) together make up 86% of everything Snap acted on. The one clean caveat is that these are self-reported counts of actions, not of violations or of people — a single account can be enforced many times, and Snap's own category definitions decide what lands where.

  • Data: snap_metrics (Overview of Our T&S Enforcements; United States & International Government Information Requests; Combating CSEA), Transparency Report API, periods 2024-H1 and 2024-H2.

The category mix is dominated by four buckets

Snap's global enforcement is not spread evenly across its thirteen violation categories. Sexual Content alone drew more actions than the bottom eleven categories combined. The table below shows 2024-H2 enforcement volumes, each category's share of the total, and Snap's reported median turnaround — the gap between the fastest and slowest categories is the more revealing story.

Two patterns stand out. First, the volume is concentrated where automated detection is easiest: sexual imagery and spam are pattern-matchable, and Snap clears them in well under two minutes. Second, the categories that require human judgment take longest — Hate Speech (26.6 minutes) and Child Sexual Exploitation (23.4 minutes) are the two slowest, despite CSE carrying the heaviest legal stakes. Speed appears to track ambiguity, not severity.

Against the prior half-year, total enforcements rose modestly (9.70M → 10.03M, +3.4%), but the movement underneath was larger. Harassment and Bullying jumped 19% and Self-Harm & Suicide enforcement grew 2.4x (6,509 → 15,910), while Drugs fell 19% and Child Sexual Exploitation fell 18%. Snap separately reports its CSEA program submitting 417,842 reports to NCMEC in 2024-H2, down from 632,436 in 2024-H1 — a 34% drop that could reflect fewer violations, a detection change, or both, and shouldn't be read either way without more periods.

The US government is Snap's single biggest data requester

Enforcement is one lever; responding to law enforcement is another. In 2024-H2 Snap reports 24,246 US government information requests naming 40,251 accounts — more requests than every other country in the world combined (20,445 international requests, led by France at 6,208 and Germany at 5,705).

Snap produced at least some data in roughly four of five requests across most process types. The lowest compliance rate is for Emergency Disclosure Requests (71%) — the one channel with no court oversight, where a provider decides for itself whether an emergency is genuine. That is exactly where you'd expect a platform to refuse most often, and the data bears it out.

Caveats

Every figure here is self-reported by Snap and covers only two half-year periods (2024-H1 and 2024-H2), so "trends" are single step-changes, not established directions. Enforcement counts are actions, not distinct violations or users, and are not additive across the other tables — the T&S "Child Sexual Exploitation" figure (961,359 actions) uses a different definition from the dedicated CSEA section's content-enforced count (1,228,929) and should not be conflated. The "some data produced" percentages are rates and cannot be summed or averaged across rows. Turnaround times are medians, which say nothing about the slowest cases. Category definitions and detection systems are Snap's own and can shift between reports.
