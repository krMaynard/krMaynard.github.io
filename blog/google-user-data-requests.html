Government Demands for Google User Data Grew 25-Fold. The U.S. No Longer Dominates.

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

From 12,539 requests in late 2009 to 313,424 in the first half of 2025, and India now files almost as many as the United States, while the fastest-growing demander is Brazil.

Bottom line: Across sixteen years of Google's biannual transparency exports, worldwide government requests for user information rose from 12,539 in 2009-H2 to 313,424 in 2025-H1, roughly 25x, though a 2014-H2 reporting change (Google began publishing a per-country legal-process breakdown and counting preservation requests) inflates the jump; on a consistent post-2014 basis the rise is still about 9x. The headline caveat is composition: the United States, once 41% of all requests, is now just 26.0%, as India (23.3%), Germany (13.9%) and Brazil (12.9%) surged. These are Google's self-reported figures; the latest complete period is 2025-H1.

  • Data: google_userdata_metrics (dataset global, us_fisa_content/us_fisa_non_content/us_nsl), Transparency Report API, 2009-H2 through 2025-H1.

The curve, and the great rebalancing

Two forces drive the growth: more countries filing, and each request touching more accounts. Worldwide, accounts specified in requests reached 724,861 in 2025-H1, up from 25,342 when Google first reported them in 2011-H1. But the more striking story is who is demanding data. In 2014-H2 the U.S. filed 41% of the world's requests; by 2025-H1 that share had fallen to 26.0%, not because U.S. demands shrank (they grew 5.7x) but because everyone else grew faster.

Country 2014-H2 requests 2025-H1 requests Growth 2025-H1 share
United States 14,271 81,595 5.7x 26.0%
India 3,131 72,975 23.3x 23.3%
Germany 3,139 43,621 13.9x 13.9%
Brazil 808 40,322 49.9x 12.9%
United Kingdom 2,150 15,230 7.1x 4.9%

Those four countries account for 76.1% of all 2025-H1 requests (across 107 reporting countries). Brazil's roughly 50-fold rise and India's 23-fold rise are the engines of the global curve; the U.S. is now the slowest-growing of the major demanders. (Requests here are the sum of every non-All legal process per country; for 2009-H2 through 2014-H1 I use Google's single All total, since the process split in those years covered only the United States.)

Disclosure rates diverge sharply by process and by country

Volume is not the same as data handed over. In 2025-H1, U.S. disclosure rates ran high for court-supervised process, 92% for Other Court Orders, 89% for both Search Warrants and Subpoenas, and lower for Emergency Disclosure Requests (86%), Wiretap Orders (80%) and Pen Register Orders (75%). Across countries, the spread on ordinary legal requests is wider: the U.K. saw data produced 90% of the time, Germany 85%, France 81%, India 78%, Brazil 77%, and Russia 0%, with Google reporting no data produced for its requests at all.

What the FISA bands hide

The U.S. national-security figures are reported only as banded ranges (value_low ≠ value_high) and are non-additive, so they can't be summed with the rest. The bands conceal a remarkable trend. The number of FISA content requests has sat in the same "0–499" band for the entire sixteen years, it looks flat and small. But the accounts those requests target have exploded:

Period FISA content accounts FISA content requests
2009-H1 2,000 – 2,499 0 – 499
2014-H2 18,500 – 18,999 0 – 499
2020-H1 73,500 – 73,999 0 – 499
2025-H1 177,500 – 177,999 0 – 499

Accounts swept up by FISA content orders rose roughly 71x while the request count stayed hidden behind an unchanging "fewer than 500" band. With fewer than 500 requests reaching ~178,000 accounts, each disclosure now touches at least ~356 accounts on average, the sort of scale a small banded request count is designed not to reveal. FISA non-content orders reached 75,500–75,999 accounts in 2025-H1; National Security Letters remain comparatively tiny (500–999 requests, 1,500–1,999 accounts in 2025-H2).

Caveats. All figures are Google's self-reported transparency data; latest complete period is 2025-H1 (2025-H2 was still filling in at export time). The 2014-H2 jump is partly a reporting change: Google began decomposing requests by legal process for all countries and counting Preservation Requests, which alone were 26,410 (8.4%) of the 2025-H1 total, the pre-2014 All totals exclude them, so the full-span 25x is not strictly like-for-like. Percentages are per country × process and are never summed. National-security ranges are lower/upper bounds, not exact counts, and are reported on a delay. "Requests for data of local subsidiary" (23 in 2025-H1) can in principle overlap other categories but is negligible.

Data: google_userdata_metrics (global, us_fisa_content, us_fisa_non_content, us_nsl), Transparency Report API, 2009-H2–2025-H1.
