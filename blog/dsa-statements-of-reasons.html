From Aggregate Reports to Individual Decisions: Adding the DSA Transparency Database

July 11, 2026
EU DSA VLOP Data Engineering

The VLOP dashboard has, until now, been about the EU Digital Services Act's aggregated transparency reports — the periodic filings where a platform reports, by category, how many notices it received and how many pieces of content it actioned over six months. That's one of the DSA's two transparency mechanisms. This update adds the other one, which is a very different animal: the DSA Transparency Database of Statements of Reasons.

What a Statement of Reasons is

Under Article 17 of the DSA, whenever an in-scope platform restricts a piece of content — removes it, disables access to it, demotes it, age-restricts it, demonetizes it — it has to give the affected user a "statement of reasons" explaining the decision. And it has to file a copy of that statement to a public Transparency Database run by the European Commission, with the personal details stripped out.

The aggregated reports tell you what a platform says it did over a reporting period. The Transparency Database is the individual decisions themselves — one row per moderation action, filed as it happens, with structured fields for the category, the legal ground, whether it was detected automatically, whether the decision was automated, and what restriction was applied. It is the closest thing that exists to a real-time, decision-level record of content moderation across the EU.

It is also enormous. Platforms file on the order of a couple of billion statements a month. The full historical dump is around four terabytes and grows every day.

You can't put four terabytes in a dashboard

So the interesting engineering question was how to get from four terabytes of daily CSV dumps to something that fits behind an interactive query interface without an analytics warehouse behind it.

The answer turned out to be the Commission's own tooling. Alongside the database, the DSA team publishes an open-source Python toolbox, dsa-tdb, that downloads the daily dumps, verifies them, and — crucially — publishes pre-computed monthly aggregates. Instead of the raw statements, you can pull one row per fine-grained combination of platform, day, category, decision ground, automation, and restriction type, with a count. That's about seven megabytes of gzipped CSV per month rather than the raw firehose.

From there it's a re-aggregation problem. I roll the daily aggregates up to months, collapse them into a handful of one-dimension cuts — total statements, by category, by decision ground, by automation, by restriction type — and keep the top 60 platforms by volume, which account for about 99.97% of all statements. The result is a tidy, long-format table: roughly 24,500 rows, about two megabytes of JSON, covering September 2023 to the present. That vendors into the same read-only SQLite database as every other dataset and becomes an ordinary queryable table, reachable from the same structured, no-SQL query interface as the rest of the site.

What the shape of it looks like

The first thing you notice is how lopsided the volume is. One platform — Google Shopping — accounts for the large majority of all statements in the database, running to hundreds of millions a month, almost entirely product listings delisted as out of scope for the marketplace. TikTok, Facebook, Pinterest, Amazon, and AliExpress make up most of the rest. A handful of marketplaces and large social platforms dominate; the long tail of hundreds of smaller filers is a rounding error.

Past that, the decision-level fields let you see things the aggregated reports don't surface cleanly: the split between content restricted as illegal under law versus incompatible with the platform's own terms (overwhelmingly the latter), the share of decisions made fully automatically versus with a human in the loop, and which of the DSA's statement categories the month's decisions actually cite. The /dsa-db page charts the monthly totals, the top platforms over time, the category mix, and the illegal-versus-terms ground, with a per-platform table underneath.

The same caveats, sharper

Everything I wrote about the limitations of the aggregated reports still applies here, and a couple of new ones show up. The category definitions are still each platform's own, so cross-platform category comparisons are comparisons of platform-defined constructs, not a consistent one. The data is still self-reported and unaudited.

And the aggregation has its own traps, which the query interface warns you about. The single-dimension cuts each partition the same set of statements, so a category breakdown sums back to the platform's total — which means summing a breakdown together with the total double-counts. The restriction-type cut is multi-select, because one decision can both remove content and disable an account, so those rows don't sum to the total at all. And because a few marketplaces dominate the volume so heavily, almost any cross-platform sum is really just measuring Google Shopping. The practical rule is the same one the interface nudges you toward: pin a section and usually a platform before you aggregate anything.

Two lenses on the same law

What I like about having both datasets in one place is that they answer different questions. The aggregated reports are a platform's own summary of what it did over a period, in its own categories, reviewed by its legal and policy teams before publication. The Transparency Database is the raw stream of individual decisions, structured the same way for everyone, filed continuously. Neither is a substitute for the other, and the discrepancies between them are often the interesting part. Having them side by side, behind the same query interface, is the point.
