Are VLOP "Own-Initiative" Numbers Comparable? A Cross-Platform Audit

July 14, 2026
EU DSA VLOP Transparency Data

Part 1 showed every VLOP filing points to the broad reading of "own initiative." Part 2 asks whether the resulting numbers can be compared across platforms, or even reconciled with each platform's own second DSA disclosure. Both answers are no.

Bottom line: The DSA's own-initiative metric fails two independent comparability tests. (1) Platforms classify the same behaviour incompatibly: marketplaces file ~100% of moderation as "own initiative," while community/review platforms file the majority as user "notifications", so the denominator of "own initiative" means opposite things. (2) For a concentrated tail of platforms, the two mandated disclosures, the harmonised report (Table 5/6) and the Statements of Reasons (SoR), don't reconcile: report-to-SoR ratios run from 0.8× to 35×. And the two systems don't even cover the same services: search engines (Google Search, Bing) file the harmonised report but, as VLOSEs rather than online platforms, owe no Statements of Reasons at all under Article 24(5), putting 2.8 bn reported actions structurally outside the database. The single number "own-initiative content moderation" is not a like-for-like quantity across the ecosystem.

  • Data: dsa_tdb_metrics (SoR database, H2 2025), t5_own_initiative_illegal + t6_own_initiative_tos (harmonised VLOP reports, H2 2025). Transparency Report API.
  • Follows: Part 1, Two Readings of "Own Initiative" on DSA Table 6.

1. Platforms classify "own initiative" incompatibly

Every content-moderation SoR is tagged with a source_type: own-initiative, Article 16 notice, trusted-flagger, or other notification (a non-Article-16 user report). Sorting the 40 platforms that filed >1 M SoRs in H2 2025 by how they split that field reveals four distinct reporting philosophies:

The split is structural, not random. Marketplaces detect violations by scanning their own catalogue → they see it as own-initiative. Community platforms run on user flagging → they see the same enforcement as notification-driven. Neither is wrong under the template (Part 1 showed the "own initiative" boundary is under-defined), but it means a cross-platform sum or ranking of "own-initiative moderation" adds together numbers that were populated on incompatible definitions. Twitch's proactive enforcement and Google Shopping's proactive enforcement land in different DSA tables.

2. A platform's two DSA disclosures don't reconcile

The harmonised report (Table 5/6) and the SoR database are both mandatory, both cover H2 2025, and both purport to count own-initiative moderation. For online platforms they should be in the same ballpark per service. Most are, and a concentrated tail is not:

Two tiers emerge. A well-behaved cluster (Google Shopping, Amazon, Instagram, Maps, Facebook, Pinterest, Booking, YouTube) reconciles within ~1.5×, their report and their SoR filings tell the same story. Then a divergent tail where the two disclosures are 3×–35× apart (SHEIN, LinkedIn, Snapchat, Temu). The naive aggregate looks dramatic, reports claim 2.0× more actions than appear as SoRs (7.0 bn vs 3.4 bn), but that headline is mostly an artefact: drop the SoR-exempt search engines and it falls to 1.22×; drop Temu as well and the rest of the ecosystem reconciles at 1.03×. The finding is not a global gap but a concentrated tail.

Some of this is the legitimate measures ≠ statements gap flagged in Part 1 (one action can bundle several SoRs, or none). Unit differences can legitimately vary by product type (account-level actions bundle into statements differently on a marketplace than a social network), so some spread is expected. But 3×–35× on services whose peers reconcile at ~1.0× points to per-platform methodology choices, each drawing the boundary of "a reportable own-initiative action" somewhere different, not unit conversion alone.

The 2.7-billion-action blind spot, legal, but consequential

Google Search reports 2,703,161,113 own-initiative illegal-content actions in Table 5 (search delistings on legal grounds). The entire SoR database contains 7,578,061 illegal-ground SoRs, from all platforms combined. The explanation is legal, not scandalous: Google Search is a VLOSE, and the Article 17/24(5) statement-of-reasons machinery attaches to hosting services and online platforms, search engines file the harmonised report but owe no SoRs. (An earlier draft of this analysis framed the gap as under-filing; that was wrong.)

The consequence still matters: the SoR database is structurally blind to search-engine moderation, which happens to be the largest own-initiative line in the whole harmonised corpus. A researcher treating the database as "the record of EU content moderation" silently misses ~2.7 bn search delistings, and sees illegal-ground moderation as a rounding error (7.6 m) when the reports put it in the billions. The two disclosure systems don't just count differently, they cover different populations of services.

(Of the other zero-SoR rows: App Store's absence, 16 m reported measures, no SoR trace despite volumes far above the database's aggregation cut, is the one genuinely unexplained case. XNXX and Zalando may simply fall below the top-60 volume cut, and name-matching between the two datasets is manual, so small-service absences are weak evidence.)

3. Conclusion

Part 1 established that no VLOP files on the narrow reading of "own initiative." Part 2 shows that agreement is superficial: beneath the shared broad reading, the metric is not comparable and not self-consistent.

  • Across platforms, "own initiative" is populated on structurally different definitions, a marketplace's ~100% and a community platform's ~2% are not on the same axis.
  • Within a platform, the two mandatory disclosures diverge 3×–35× for a concentrated tail (SHEIN, LinkedIn, Snapchat, Temu) while most of the ecosystem reconciles at ~1×; and the largest own-initiative line in the harmonised corpus (Google Search, 2.7 bn) sits structurally outside the SoR database, which does not cover search engines.

The practical upshot for anyone using this data: never aggregate or rank "own-initiative" figures across platforms without first pinning source_type classification, checking whether the service is even in scope of the SoR duty, and reconciling report-vs-database per platform. The headline number is an interpretive artefact, not a measurement, which is the deeper cost of the under-defined boundary Part 1 identified.
