Are VLOP “Own-Initiative” Numbers Comparable? A Cross-Platform Audit

July 14, 2026
EU DSA VLOP Transparency Data

Part 1 showed every VLOP reads “own initiative” broadly. Part 2 asks whether the resulting numbers can be compared across platforms — or even reconciled with each platform’s own second DSA disclosure. Both answers are no.

Bottom line: The DSA's own-initiative metric fails two independent comparability tests. (1) Platforms classify the same behaviour incompatibly: marketplaces file ~100% of moderation as "own initiative," while community/review platforms file the majority as user "notifications" — so the denominator of "own initiative" means opposite things. (2) A platform's two mandated disclosures — its harmonised report (Table 5/6) and its Statements of Reasons (SoR) — don't reconcile: report-to-SoR ratios run from 0.8× to 35×, and several large services (Google Search, X, App Store) report billions or tens of millions of own-initiative actions with essentially no SoR counterpart at all. The single number "own-initiative content moderation" is not a like-for-like quantity across the ecosystem.

  • Data: dsa_tdb_metrics (SoR database, H2 2025), t5_own_initiative_illegal + t6_own_initiative_tos (harmonised VLOP reports, H2 2025). Transparency Report API.
  • Follows: Part 1 — Two Readings of "Own Initiative" on DSA Table 6.

1. Platforms classify "own initiative" incompatibly

Every content-moderation SoR is tagged with a source_type: own-initiative, Article 16 notice, trusted-flagger, or other notification (a non-Article-16 user report). Sorting the 40 platforms that filed >1 M SoRs in H2 2025 by how they split that field reveals four distinct reporting philosophies:

All-as-notification (notification ≥50%) 7 Community/review/UGC: Twitch, Quora, Trustpilot, Tripadvisor, Roblox, Kleinanzeigen, Azar

The split is structural, not random. Marketplaces detect violations by scanning their own catalogue → they see it as own-initiative. Community platforms run on user flagging → they see the same enforcement as notification-driven. Neither is wrong under the template (Part 1 showed the "own initiative" boundary is under-defined), but it means a cross-platform sum or ranking of "own-initiative moderation" adds together numbers that were populated on incompatible definitions. Twitch's proactive enforcement and Google Shopping's proactive enforcement land in different DSA tables.

2. A platform's two DSA disclosures don't reconcile

The harmonised report (Table 5/6) and the SoR database are both mandatory, both cover H2 2025, and both purport to count own-initiative moderation. They should be in the same ballpark per service. They are not — the report-to-SoR ratio spans a factor of ~45:

Two tiers emerge. A well-behaved cluster (Google Shopping, Amazon, Instagram, Maps, Facebook, Pinterest, Booking, YouTube) reconciles within ~1.5× — their report and their SoR filings tell the same story. Then a divergent tail where the two disclosures are 3×–35× apart (SHEIN, LinkedIn, Snapchat, Temu). In aggregate across matched services, the harmonised reports claim 2.0× more own-initiative actions than appear as SoRs (7.0 bn vs 3.4 bn).

Some of this is the legitimate measures ≠ statements gap flagged in Part 1 (one action can bundle several SoRs, or none). But a consistent unit difference would produce a constant ratio; a ratio that swings from 0.8× to 35× across platforms is a methodology difference, not a unit conversion — each platform is drawing the boundary of "a reportable own-initiative action" somewhere different.

The headline exhibit: Google Search's 2.7-billion-action gap

Google Search reports **2,703,161,113 own-initiative illegal-content actions in Table 5 (search delistings on legal grounds). The entire SoR database contains 7,578,061 illegal-ground SoRs** — from all platforms combined. Google Search's illegal-content moderation, the single largest own-initiative line in the harmonised reports, is ~360× larger than every illegal-ground SoR on the database put together, and files essentially none of it as a Statement of Reasons. A researcher reading only the SoR database would conclude illegal-content own-initiative moderation is a rounding error; a researcher reading only the harmonised reports would conclude it is the dominant activity on the internet. Both are reading official DSA disclosures.

*("No SoRs filed" also covers X — which classifies its few SoRs as notification, §1 — and App Store/Bing/Zalando/XNXX, which either file under a different classification or below the database's top-60 volume cut. For Google Search, X and App Store the volumes are far above any cut, so the absence is real.)*

3. Conclusion

Part 1 established that VLOPs agree on how broadly to read "own initiative" (all of them: broadly). Part 2 shows that agreement is superficial: beneath the shared broad reading, the metric is not comparable and not self-consistent.

  • Across platforms, "own initiative" is populated on structurally different definitions — a marketplace's ~100% and a community platform's ~2% are not on the same axis.
  • Within a platform, the two mandatory disclosures diverge by up to 35×, and the largest single line of own-initiative moderation in the entire harmonised corpus (Google Search, 2.7 bn) has no SoR footprint at all.

The practical upshot for anyone using this data: never aggregate or rank "own-initiative" figures across platforms without first pinning source_type classification and reconciling report-vs-database per platform. The headline number is an interpretive artefact, not a measurement — which is the deeper cost of the under-defined boundary Part 1 identified.
