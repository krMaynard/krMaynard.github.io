What Korea's Nth Room Law Extracts From Google

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

Under the transparency rule born of the Nth Room scandal, Google reports 442,452 URLs flagged as illegal sexual content in Korea across six annual reports, and deepfakes account for just 0.6% of the requests.

Bottom line: Google's Korea Network Act reports show flagged-URL volume exploding from 61 in 2020 to 158,052 in 2024 before easing to 115,280 in 2025, with roughly four in five requests filed by government-side agencies rather than victims, but the series has a hard caveat: child/youth sexual-abuse requests collapse from 29,821 in 2024 to 927 in 2025 at an exact report-year boundary, which looks like a reporting or routing change, not a real-world drop.

  • Data: korea_network_act_metrics, Transparency Report API, annual reports 2020–2025 (monthly detail 2024–2025).

A law that makes platforms count

After the Nth Room digital-sex-crime scandal, Korea amended its Network Act (Art. 64-5) and Telecommunications Business Act (Art. 22-5) to force online service providers to take technical measures against illegally filmed content, "fake" (deepfake) imagery, and child/youth sexual-abuse material, and to publish an annual report about it. Unlike general transparency reports, this one is scoped to a single harm and, since the 2024 report, broken down monthly by complainant, reason, and outcome.

Google (covering Search and YouTube jointly) reports six years of headline figures:

Year URLs received URLs removed Removal rate
2020 61 42 68.9%
2021 31,281 18,294 58.5%
2022 47,162 38,908 82.5%
2023 90,616 81,593 90.0%
2024 158,052 142,211 90.0%
2025 115,280 92,334 80.1%

The 2020 figure is an artifact of timing, the obligations took effect only at the end of 2020, so the first report covers a startup sliver. From the first full year, volume grew five-fold to the 2024 peak (31,281 → 158,052), then fell 27% in 2025. The dataset also carries annual summaries from Kakao and Naver, and the contrast is stark: over the same six years Kakao reports 462 URLs received (391 removed) and Naver 325 (91 removed), Google accounts for essentially all of the volume in this dataset, so any "Korean platforms" aggregate here is really a Google number.

Who asks, and about what

The 2024–2025 monthly detail (273,332 requests) shows this is mostly a government-to-platform pipeline: agencies and organizations filed 217,446 requests (79.6%) versus 55,886 from victims and other users (20.4%). By reason, the law's original target dominates:

Request reason (2024–2025) Requests Share URLs removed
Illegal photos and videos 240,970 88.2% 206,770
Child or youth sexual-abuse content 30,748 11.2% 26,923
Fake images and videos (deepfakes) 1,614 0.6% 852

The deepfake row is the surprise. Korea's 2024 deepfake crisis made "fake images and videos" the law's most-discussed category, yet Google reports only 964 deepfake requests in all of 2024 and 650 in 2025. There is a visible spike, 517 requests in June 2024 alone, plus 183 in August and 118 in September as the school-deepfake scandal peaked, but even then the category never approaches the volume of ordinary illegally filmed content. Deepfake requests also end in removal least often: 852 removed against 1,614 requested (~53%), versus ~86% for illegal photos/videos and ~88% for CSAM. When Google reports not removing content, the dominant stated reason is "content not found," which grew from 7,508 URLs in 2024 to 12,841 in 2025.

The CSAM cliff is a definition change

The most dramatic line in the data is child/youth sexual-abuse content: a steady 1,300–3,700 requests every month of 2024 (29,821 total), then 3 in January 2025, 1 in February, and only 927 for the whole year. A 97% one-year drop in CSAM reporting would be extraordinary, and the cliff lands exactly at the boundary between the 2024 and 2025 reports while other categories move smoothly, which is the signature of a classification or intake-channel change (for instance, CSAM being routed or counted outside this request stream), not of the problem disappearing. Treat the 2025 category mix accordingly.

Caveats. All figures are self-reported by the platforms in their statutory reports; "Google reports" is doing real work here. The 2020 numbers cover only a partial startup period, and the latest report covers calendar 2025. The four monthly sections are cross-cuts of the same requests, so requests received, reasons, and removal outcomes were never summed across sections. Requests and URLs are nearly 1:1 in this data (273,332 requests vs 273,324 processed URLs) but are formally different units. The 2025 CSAM collapse is almost certainly a reporting-definition break; six-year totals are Google-dominated, with Kakao and Naver contributing under 800 URLs combined.

Data: korea_network_act_metrics, Transparency Report API, 2020–2025 (monthly detail 2024–2025).
