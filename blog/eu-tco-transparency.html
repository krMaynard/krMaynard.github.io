Europe's One-Hour Terror Takedown Law Runs on 349 Orders — and One Country

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

The EU's Terrorist Content Online Regulation lets authorities order content removed within one hour. In its first 19 months, all of Europe issued 349 removal orders — 249 of them from Germany alone.

Bottom line: The Commission's implementation report for Regulation 2021/784 counts 349 removal orders issued EU-wide from June 2022 through December 2023, from just 6 of 27 Member States — and Germany accounts for 71% of them, so the ex-Germany total is 100 orders across five countries in 19 months. The platforms' own TCO transparency reports show the receiving end is just as thin: across every platform-year in this dataset, 57 pieces of content were removed under removal orders, while the same reports describe proactive removals in the millions (Google alone reports 181.3 million items removed proactively in 2025, under its report's own, broader-than-orders scope).

  • Data: tco_metrics, Transparency Report API, authority stream 2022-06..2023-12 plus Ireland 2024; platform reports 2022–2025.

349 orders, six countries, one dominant issuer

The TCO Regulation's headline power is the Article 3 removal order: a designated national authority tells a hosting provider to take terrorist content down within one hour. The Commission's aggregation of Member State reports shows how rarely that power was actually used. Twenty-three Member States had designated a competent authority by end-2023, but only six ever issued an order:

The per-country figures sum exactly to the EU total, so nothing is hidden in an "other" bucket. Strip out Germany and the entire rest of the EU issued about five orders a month. The one national regulator with its own row in this dataset, Ireland's Coimisiún na Meán, reports for 2024: three Article 5(4) decisions finding a provider "exposed to terrorist content," zero penalties, zero review proceedings.

What the platforms say they received

The Article 7 side — platforms' own annual TCO reports — is consistent with a regime that barely touches the biggest services. Google reports zero removal orders received in both 2022 and 2023, the very window in which authorities say they issued 349. Google reports 3 orders in 2024 and 12 items removed via orders in 2025. Meta reports that Facebook received 143 removal-order requests in 2023 — but counted only 15 as valid orders, removing 10 pieces of content as a result. Spotify's 2024 report, across its four sub-services, shows 35 items removed via orders. If the big platforms received almost none of the 349, most orders evidently went to smaller hosting providers that don't appear here.

The proactive machine dwarfs the legal one

Each report's "proactive" figure follows its own definitions — Google's covers its services' broader violent-extremism enforcement and grew from 1.4 million items in 2022 to 181.3 million in 2025, a 129-fold rise; Meta's ~6.1 million spans three policy areas wider than terrorism alone; Spotify's terrorism-only count is 663. So the numbers can't be summed across companies. But the shape is unmistakable and holds within every single report: the legally compelled channel removes items in the tens, the voluntary channel in the millions. The same asymmetry shows up in error-correction volumes: Meta reports ~857,200 appeals with ~156,000 items reinstated (about 18%), and Google reports 215,840 appeals in 2025 with 18,506 decisions reversed (about 8.6%) — each platform's appeals alone exceed the EU's entire order count by three to four orders of magnitude.

Caveats. All figures are self-reported: authority counts come from the Commission's first implementation report (covering June 2022–December 2023, a 19-month window — the latest available on that stream), platform figures from each company's own TCO report, with periods that don't align (Meta's is calendar 2023, Google's runs to 2025). Metric scope differs by publisher — proactive-removal counts are not comparable across companies, and Meta's appeal and reinstatement figures are rounded approximations. Composition matters throughout: Germany alone is 71% of all orders, and Google dominates any proactive-removal aggregate. Orders and items are different units — one order can cover multiple pieces of content — so the 349 orders and 57 items removed via orders are related but not directly divisible.
