China's Internet Complaint Machine: 1.26 Billion Reports and Counting

July 14, 2026
Transparency Data

Since October 2019, China's 12377 reporting system says it has taken in 1.26 billion public reports of "illegal and harmful" online information — about 19 million a month lately, and more than 90% of them are handled by the platforms themselves, not the government.

Bottom line: The CAC's Illegal and Harmful Information Reporting Center (12377) reports 1,263.1 million public reports received across 79 monthly bulletins from October 2019 through May 2026 — a volume with no Western analogue — and the trailing-twelve-month total (225.3 million, June 2025–May 2026) is 46% above the system's first twelve months. The biggest caveat is baked into the headline number: 91.5% of those reports were received by "major websites and platforms," so the national total is overwhelmingly a tally of platforms' own complaint channels that the CAC aggregates and publishes, not reports filed with a government hotline. Its predecessor-era sibling, the 12321 telecom-spam hotline, published a comparable monthly bulletin from September 2016 until it went silent after February 2019 — at roughly one-hundredth the volume.

  • Data: ciirc_metrics and china12321_metrics, Transparency Report API; monthly bulletins 2019-10 → 2026-05 (CIIRC/12377) and 2016-09 → 2019-02 (12321).

Nineteen million reports a month, and rising

The 12377 series starts at 10.0 million reports in October 2019 and never looks back. Annual totals climb from 163.2 million (2020) to 223.3 million (2025); the single biggest month on record is January 2026, at 22.1 million. Comparing like-for-like twelve-month windows, the first year of the series (Oct 2019–Sep 2020) logged 154.4 million reports against 225.3 million in the most recent year — growth of 46%, with most of the step-up concentrated in 2023, when volumes jumped 19% year over year.

Who actually handles the reports

The bulletin splits its national total into three receiving bodies, and the split is lopsided: over the full series, platforms account for 91.5%, local cyberspace-affairs departments 6.0%, and the CAC's own central reporting center just 2.5% (31.2 million reports in six and a half years). The platform share has also drifted steadily upward — from 88.8% in 2020 to 93.6% in 2025 — while the local-department share fell from 9.8% to 4.1%. Since January 2025 the bulletins add a "major commercial platforms" sub-line, and it shows the platform figure is itself concentrated: commercial platforms received 89–93% of all platform-channel reports in most months disclosed. Read plainly, China's headline "national reporting" statistic is mostly a census of complaint buttons on large commercial apps and websites, with government channels a thin layer on top.

The spam hotline that went quiet

The 12321 center — run under the Internet Society of China/MIIT, with a telecom-nuisance remit rather than a content one — published 26 bulletins covering September 2016 through February 2019, then stopped. Summed across its disjoint report categories, the series totals about 4.78 million reports over 29 covered months, roughly 165,000 a month — about 1% of 12377's monthly volume. Its internal mix inverted over that short life: in late 2016 the hotline was dominated by reports about mobile apps, but by 2018 harassment phone calls and bad websites had taken over.

12321 category Dec 2016 Dec 2018
Mobile apps 129,493 50,440
Harassment calls 16,728 86,000
Bad websites 23,635 72,000
SMS (spam + illegal) 16,076 36,000

The data ends there: the February 2019 bulletin (137,000 reports) is the last, and the series itself says nothing about why publication ceased.

Caveats. Everything here is self-reported by the publishing agencies; "X reports" is the honest verb throughout. Figures are published in units of ten thousand (万), so anything below that threshold is invisible, and 12321's 2017+ bulletins are rounded approximations while its 2016 bulletins are exact counts — the Dec 2016 vs Dec 2018 comparison mixes the two. Coverage has holes (no Oct 2024 CIIRC bulletin; no Oct 2017 for 12321; three 12321 bulletins combine two months). The two systems have different remits — harmful content vs telecom spam — so the ~100× volume gap partly reflects scope, not just uptake. The bulletins disclose no per-platform, per-category, or outcome data, so "reports received" says nothing about what happened to the content. Latest data: May 2026 (CIIRC), February 2019 (12321).
