What the EU AI Act's Training-Data Summaries Do and Don't Reveal

July 9, 2026
EU AI Act AI/LLM Transparency Data

Article 53(1)(d) of the EU AI Act requires every provider of a general-purpose AI model to publish a "sufficiently detailed summary" of the content used to train it, on a template the AI Office released in July 2025. The obligation took effect on 2 August 2025. I've been collecting the summaries that have actually been filed into a small, comparable dataset — it's live at transparency.kieranmaynard.com/ai-training — and the exercise turns out to say more about the state of the regulation than about the models.

What the template actually asks for

The template is deliberately modest. For each modality present in the training data — Text, Image, Audio, Video, Other — the provider ticks one of three size bands (for text: less than 1 billion tokens, 1 billion to 10 trillion, or more than 10 trillion) and describes the type of content. Then a set of yes/no flags for where the data came from: publicly available datasets, commercially licensed data, private data from third parties, data the provider crawled itself, user data, and synthetic data. Plus a data-acquisition cut-off date.

That's it. No token counts, no dataset names, no proportions. The Commission designed it to give copyright holders and the public a baseline without forcing providers to hand over trade secrets. So the ceiling on what any single summary can tell you is set low by design. The value, if there is any, comes from putting them side by side — which is the thing nobody else was doing, and the reason I built the dataset. I normalize each size band to a rank (1–3, with 0 for "not applicable") so the coarse sizes at least sort across providers.

The most informative signal is who's missing

Eleven models from seven providers had filed the standardized template when I last refreshed the data: Google (Gemini 3 Pro), Meta (Muse Spark), Microsoft (the Phi-4 family), OpenAI (GPT-5.5), Switzerland's Swiss AI (Apertus), Poland's SpeakLeash (Bielik), and Hugging Face (SmolLM3).

The conspicuous absences are the frontier labs you'd expect to see. Anthropic — including its current flagship, Fable 5 — Mistral, and xAI have not filed on the template. They disclose training content the old way, as a paragraph of prose in a model card: "a proprietary mix of publicly available information, licensed data, and data generated by our teams," or words to that effect. That's a real disclosure, but it isn't the standardized artifact the regulation now asks for, and it isn't comparable to anything.

The divide isn't defiance versus compliance. It's a timing seam written into the law. Models placed on the EU market after 2 August 2025 have to file now; models already on the market before that date have until 2 August 2027 to produce their summary. So the models on the template are, almost entirely, either brand-new flagships that crossed the line after the cutoff, or open-source and European labs that filed proactively because openness is their whole pitch. The abstainers are mostly sitting on the transitional deadline. "Who has filed" is currently a better proxy for "who shipped a new model recently, or wants to look transparent" than for "who is more transparent."

What the filed data does show

Read across the summaries and a few things hold up.

Everyone claims public data; the interesting variation is everywhere else. Every filer ticks "publicly available datasets." That flag carries almost no information — it's the price of entry. Where providers actually differ is the other categories: whether they ran their own crawler or leaned on public datasets someone else assembled, whether they licensed data commercially, whether they used synthetic data, and — the one that reads as a genuine disclosure — whether they trained on user data. OpenAI ticks user data yes, and the summary is careful to say it means data from products like ChatGPT and Codex, not model-interaction logs; Meta ticks it yes too. The open and European labs — Apertus, Bielik, SmolLM3 — tick it no, alongside no commercial licensing and no self-crawling. The template's yes/no flags are blunt, but that particular column is where a summary tells you something a marketing page wouldn't.

The top size band has already saturated. "More than 10 trillion tokens" is the largest bucket the template offers for text, and it's simply the norm now: Gemini 3 Pro, Muse Spark, GPT-5.5, and SmolLM3 all sit in it. A 3-billion-parameter open model and a frontier system report the same text-size band. That's not an error — it's the band doing exactly what a three-bucket scale does once the field has moved past the top bucket. It stops discriminating. The size fields are useful for separating a small or older model (Bielik lands in the middle band) from a modern large one, and useless for anything finer.

Modality coverage is the cleanest cut. The one thing the data separates crisply is multimodal versus text-only. Google, Meta, and OpenAI report Image, Audio, and Video training data; the open-lab entries are text-only. That's a real, verifiable distinction, and it's visible at a glance in a way the size bands aren't.

The limitations are structural, not incidental

As with the DSA transparency reports I've written about before, the constraints matter as much as the content, and several are baked into the regime rather than into my collection of it.

There is no registry. Nobody maintains a canonical list of filed summaries, so "who has published" is itself a research question — I found several of these by working backward from an academic survey and from Hugging Face model cards. Each provider self-publishes wherever it likes, in whatever format it likes. In practice that meant a transparency-report PDF from Google, form-field PDFs from Meta and OpenAI, a markdown "data summary card" from Microsoft, a PDF in a Hugging Face repo for Apertus, and — for SmolLM3 — an interactive widget backed by a Gradio app, from which I had to lift the filled template out of the application source. A standardized template published five different ways is only half-standardized.

There is no audit. Every flag is self-asserted; nothing external confirms that a "no" on crawled data or user data is accurate. And the template asks whether a source was used, never how much, so a provider that trained overwhelmingly on licensed data and a provider that touched a single licensed set both tick the same box. "Publicly available" is never defined, so it absorbs everything from curated research corpora to the open web.

None of this makes the data useless. It makes it what it is: a structured, self-reported, cross-provider snapshot that's genuinely useful for spotting the shape of the field — who trains on what kinds of sources, roughly how big, in which modalities — and misleading if you treat a ticked box as a measured quantity.

What to expect

Enforcement powers attach on 2 August 2026 — the AI Office can act on a missing or inadequate summary from that date, with penalties that run up to 3% of worldwide turnover or €15 million. The bigger shift comes with the 2 August 2027 transitional deadline, when the pre-existing models have to file too. That's when the dataset gets interesting: the flagships currently disclosing in prose — the ones I have to leave out today — will have to produce the standardized artifact or risk enforcement, and the "who's missing" signal will finally mean something closer to non-compliance than to timing.

I'd expect two things to become visible as that happens. First, the coarse top band will get more embarrassing, not less: as more frontier systems pile into "more than 10 trillion tokens," the pressure to add finer buckets — or to report an actual number — will grow, because a transparency instrument that can't distinguish a 12-trillion-token model from a 50-trillion-token one isn't telling regulators much. Second, the format fragmentation will get worse before it gets better unless the AI Office stands up an actual registry; a hundred more summaries scattered across a hundred corporate CDNs and model repos is not a transparency regime, it's a scavenger hunt.

The template is a real improvement over the prior status quo, which was nothing. But the interesting story in the data right now isn't what any model discloses — it's the seam between the labs that had to file and the labs that didn't yet, and how much that single timing accident currently explains. I'll keep the dataset updated as the 2027 deadline pulls the rest of the field onto the template, and we'll see whether comparability survives contact with a hundred more filings.
