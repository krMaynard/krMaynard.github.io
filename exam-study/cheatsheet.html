Foundations

Ch. 1–2 · always tested

Fair Information Practices (FIPs)

  • Rights of individuals — notice, access, choice
  • Controls on information — security, data quality
  • Information life cycle — collection, use, retention, disclosure
  • Management — accountability, oversight
  • Canon: OECD Guidelines (1980), Convention 108, HEW 1973 report

Four Models of Data Protection

  • Comprehensive — one omnibus law (EU/GDPR)
  • Sectoral — patchwork by industry (the U.S. approach)
  • Co-/self-regulatory — codes of conduct, seal programs
  • Technology-based — privacy protected by tools (encryption)

Sources of U.S. Privacy Law

  • Constitutional — 1st/4th Amend.; limits government, not private actors
  • Legislative — federal & state statutes
  • Regulatory — agency rules (FTC, HHS, FCC)
  • Common law — the 4 privacy torts (Prosser)
  • Preemption — when federal law displaces state law (or sets a floor)

Processing Roles & Choice

  • Controller decides purposes/means; processor acts on its behalf
  • Data subject — the identified individual
  • Opt-in (affirmative consent) vs opt-out (default yes)
  • PII vs de-identified: pseudonymized reverses, anonymized does not

Core Federal Sector Laws

the high-yield block
FCRA15 U.S.C. §1681
Fair Credit Reporting Act (1970) · amended by FACTA (2003)
Covers
Consumer reporting agencies, furnishers & users of consumer reports (credit, eligibility).
Trigger
Using a report for a permissible purpose (credit, employment, insurance).
Key rule
Employment screening needs disclosure + written authorization; adverse action requires pre- & post-notice.
Enforcer
FTC & CFPB; private right of action.
GLBA15 U.S.C. §6801
Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (1999) — financial privacy
Covers
Financial institutions & their nonpublic personal information (NPI).
Trigger
Sharing NPI with nonaffiliated third parties.
Key rule
Privacy Rule (notice + opt-out) + Safeguards Rule (security program); pretexting banned.
Enforcer
FTC, CFPB, banking regulators; no private right of action.
HIPAA42 U.S.C. §1320d
Health Insurance Portability & Accountability Act (1996) · HITECH (2009)
Covers
Covered entities (plans, clearinghouses, providers billing electronically) + business associates.
Trigger
Use/disclosure of PHI (protected health information).
Key rule
Privacy, Security & Breach Notification Rules; breach notice within 60 days.
Enforcer
HHS Office for Civil Rights; state AGs (via HITECH).
COPPA15 U.S.C. §6501
Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (1998)
Covers
Operators of sites/services directed to children under 13, or with actual knowledge.
Trigger
Collecting personal info from a child under 13.
Key rule
Verifiable parental consent before collection; clear privacy notice.
Enforcer
FTC & state AGs.
FERPA20 U.S.C. §1232g
Family Educational Rights & Privacy Act (1974)
Covers
Schools & institutions receiving federal funds; education records.
Trigger
Disclosing records without consent (rights transfer to student at 18 / postsecondary).
Key rule
Parents/students get access & amendment; directory-info opt-out.
Enforcer
Dept. of Education; funding withdrawal, no private right of action.
TCPA47 U.S.C. §227
Telephone Consumer Protection Act (1991)
Covers
Calls, texts, faxes via autodialers/prerecorded voice.
Trigger
Marketing contact without prior express (written) consent.
Key rule
Consent + do-not-call honoring; opt-in regime.
Enforcer
FCC; strong private right of action ($500–$1,500/call).
CAN-SPAM15 U.S.C. §7701
Controlling the Assault of Non-Solicited Pornography & Marketing (2003)
Covers
Senders of commercial email.
Trigger
Sending — no prior consent needed (opt-out regime).
Key rule
Honest headers, valid physical address, working opt-out honored within 10 business days.
Enforcer
FTC; no private right of action (ISPs may sue).
TSR / DNC16 C.F.R. §310
Telemarketing Sales Rule & National Do-Not-Call Registry
Covers
Telemarketers & sellers making outbound calls.
Trigger
Calling a registered number / abandoning calls.
Key rule
Scrub against the DNC Registry; disclosures; established-business-relationship exception.
Enforcer
FTC & FCC.
ECPA18 U.S.C. §2510
Electronic Communications Privacy Act (1986)
Covers
Interception & access to electronic communications.
Trigger
Wiretap Act: intercepting in transit. SCA: accessing stored comms.
Key rule
One-party consent (federal); business/provider exceptions.
Enforcer
DOJ; private right of action.
FTC §515 U.S.C. §45
FTC Act — the de facto federal privacy enforcer
Covers
Almost all commerce (banks, common carriers, nonprofits excepted).
Trigger
Unfair OR deceptive acts or practices — e.g. breaking your own privacy policy.
Key rule
Deception = misleading a reasonable consumer; unfairness = substantial, unavoidable, un-outweighed harm.
Enforcer
FTC via consent decrees (often 20-yr).
VPPA · DPPA18 U.S.C. §2710 / §2721
Video Privacy Protection Act · Driver's Privacy Protection Act
Covers
VPPA: video/streaming rental records. DPPA: state DMV records.
Trigger
Disclosing viewing history / motor-vehicle records without consent.
Key rule
Narrow, subject-specific consent regimes.
Enforcer
Private right of action (both).
Workplace stackmulti-statute
Employment privacy is layered, not one law
Hiring
FCRA background checks; EPPA (polygraph limits); GINA (genetic); ADA.
On the job
ECPA monitoring; NLRA §7 protects concerted activity.
Key rule
Reduce reasonable expectation of privacy via clear policy & notice.
Enforcer
EEOC, DOL, NLRB, FTC.

State Laws & Breach Notification

fast-moving · high-yield

Comprehensive State Privacy Laws

  • CCPA/CPRA (California) — know, delete, correct, opt-out of sale/sharing; enforced by the CPPA + AG
  • VCDPA (Virginia), CPA (Colorado), CTDPA, UCPA — controller/processor model
  • Common rights: access, deletion, portability, opt-out of targeted ads & sale
  • Mostly no private right of action (CCPA: limited, for breaches)

Breach Notification (all 50 states)

  • Triggered by unauthorized acquisition of personal information (name + SSN, financial, etc.)
  • Risk-of-harm threshold in many states; some are per-se
  • Notify affected residents; often the state AG & credit bureaus above a count
  • Timing "without unreasonable delay" — some fixed (e.g. 30/45/60 days)

Data Security & Destruction

  • State data security laws require "reasonable" safeguards (e.g. MA 201 CMR 17)
  • Data destruction laws mandate secure disposal of records
  • NY SHIELD Act broadened both breach + security duties

Government & Civil Litigation

  • e-Discovery — preserve/produce ESI; privacy vs. relevance
  • Gov't access to data: ECPA/SCA, subpoenas, warrants; CLOUD Act cross-border
  • Privacy Act of 1974 governs federal agency records

International & Cross-Border

Ch. 14 · GDPR touchpoints

GDPR Essentials (for U.S. companies)

  • Extraterritorial: applies to processing EU residents' data
  • Lawful bases (incl. consent, legitimate interest), DSAR rights
  • Fines up to 4% of global turnover / €20M

Transfer Mechanisms

  • Adequacy decisions
  • Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) & Binding Corporate Rules
  • EU–U.S. Data Privacy Framework (DPF, 2023) — replaced Privacy Shield

Know the History

  • Safe Harbor → invalidated by Schrems I
  • Privacy Shield → invalidated by Schrems II (2020)
  • DPF is the current bridge; SCCs still need a transfer impact assessment

Exam traps

  1. The Constitution limits government, not private companies — a private employer isn't bound by the 4th Amendment.
  2. CAN-SPAM is opt-out and needs no prior consent; TCPA is opt-in. Don't swap them.
  3. GLBA and CAN-SPAM have no private right of action; FCRA, TCPA, VPPA, ECPA do.
  4. HIPAA covers covered entities & business associates — not every entity holding health data (a fitness app usually isn't covered).
  5. COPPA = under 13, not 18. "Actual knowledge" also triggers it.
  6. The FTC is the primary federal privacy cop, acting through §5 "unfair or deceptive," usually via consent decrees — there's no single U.S. omnibus privacy statute.
  7. Breaking your own posted privacy policy is the classic FTC "deceptive" case.
  8. Preemption cuts both ways: some federal laws set a floor states can exceed; others fully preempt.
