Welcome to Chapter One, "Introduction to Privacy." By the end of this lecture, you should be able to recall four things: how privacy is defined and the four classes of privacy, what the Fair Information Practices are and the major frameworks that codified them, the difference between personal and nonpersonal information along with the key processing roles, and finally the four world models of data protection — comprehensive, sectoral, self-regulatory or co-regulatory, and technology-based. This chapter is foundational, so I want you to lock in vocabulary, because the exam loves to test whether you can tell two similar-sounding concepts apart.

Let's start with the definition of privacy. In 1890, Samuel Warren and Louis Brandeis published an article called "The Right to Privacy" in the Harvard Law Review, and they gave us the classic definition of privacy as "the right to be let alone." Memorize that phrase and that pairing — Warren and Brandeis, 1890, "the right to be let alone." Beyond that famous line, privacy has also been defined as the desire of people to freely choose the circumstances and the degree to which they expose their attitudes and behavior to others. It's tied to human personality, independence, dignity, and integrity.

Now, the first big framework to memorize is the four classes of privacy. First, information privacy, which is about the rules governing collection and handling of personal information — things like financial data, medical data, government records, and your activity online. This is the class the entire book focuses on. Second, bodily privacy, which concerns a person's physical being — genetic testing, drug testing, body cavity searches, and issues like birth control, abortion, and adoption. Third, territorial privacy, which places limits on intruding into someone's environment. And here's a common exam trap: "environment" is not just the home — it also includes the workplace and public spaces, and the intrusion usually looks like video surveillance, identification checks, and similar monitoring technology. Fourth, communications privacy, which protects the means of correspondence — postal mail, telephone, email, and other communicative behavior. So if the exam describes video surveillance in a public square, that's territorial; if it describes wiretapping a phone call, that's communications.

Let me touch on historical origins, because a few facts here are testable. Privacy shows up in classical Greece, the Bible, Jewish law, and the Qur'an. In England, the Justices of the Peace Act of 1361 called for arresting "peeping Toms" and eavesdroppers. In 1765, British Lord Camden struck down a warrant to enter a home and seize papers, protecting the privacy of the home. Now, the U.S. Constitution, ratified in 1789, never uses the word "privacy," but several amendments relate to it. Know these pairings cold: the Third Amendment bans quartering soldiers in your home; the Fourth Amendment generally requires a search warrant before police enter a home or business; the Fifth Amendment protects against being compelled to testify against yourself; and the Fourteenth Amendment requires due process, including for intrusions into bodily autonomy. Here's a contrast the exam likes: the federal Constitution has no explicit privacy right, but the California Constitution does — the people of California added an explicit right to privacy by ballot measure in November 1974. On the international side, in December 1948 the United Nations General Assembly adopted the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, saying no one shall be subjected to arbitrary interference with his privacy, family, home, or correspondence. Then in 1950, the Council of Europe set forth the European Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms, and Article 8 of that Convention protects respect for private and family life, home, and correspondence.

Quick recap: privacy is "the right to be let alone" from Warren and Brandeis in 1890; the four classes are information, bodily, territorial, and communications; the U.S. Constitution protects privacy only implicitly through amendments, while California's constitution does so explicitly.

Now let's turn to the heart of this chapter for exam purposes: Fair Information Practices, abbreviated FIPs, also called Fair Information Privacy Practices or Principles, abbreviated FIPPs. Since the 1970s, these have been the main way of organizing individual rights and organizational responsibilities around personal data. The definitions vary by time and place, but the themes are consistent. Conceptually, FIPs fall into four buckets. First, rights of individuals — that's notice, choice and consent, and data subject access. Second, controls on the information — that's information security and information quality. Third, the information life cycle — collection, use and retention, and disclosure. And fourth, management — meaning management and administration, plus monitoring and enforcement. If you can remember those four buckets, you can reconstruct most of what any FIPs framework says.

Now here is where you need to know the specific codifications and their dates, because the exam tests them. The earliest is the 1973 U.S. Department of Health, Education and Welfare report — its Code of Fair Information Practices is the ancestor of modern FIPs. Its core ideas: there must be no secret recordkeeping systems; a person must be able to find out what information is held and how it's used; information collected for one purpose can't be used for another without consent; a person must be able to correct records; and organizations must ensure data reliability and prevent misuse.

The second codification is the 1980 Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development Guidelines — that's the OECD Guidelines, on the Protection of Privacy and Transborder Flows of Personal Data. Updated in 2013, these are the most widely recognized FIPs framework, and importantly, they were endorsed by the U.S. Federal Trade Commission, the FTC. The OECD gives eight principles, and you should be able to name them: Collection Limitation, Data Quality, Purpose Specification, Use Limitation, Security Safeguards, Openness, Individual Participation, and Accountability. A tip for the exam — Purpose Specification means you state the purpose no later than the time of collection; Use Limitation means you don't use the data beyond that purpose except with consent or by authority of law. Those two are commonly confused: specification is about declaring the purpose up front, limitation is about sticking to it afterward.

The third is the 1981 Council of Europe Convention for the Protection of Individuals with Regard to Automatic Processing of Personal Data, known as Convention 108. This one is a binding treaty — signatory member states had to write these protections into their domestic law. Convention 108 covers quality of data, special categories of data, data security, and transborder data flows. Note that special categories concept — data revealing racial origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, health, sex life, or criminal convictions cannot be automatically processed unless domestic law provides safeguards. In 2018 came an update called Convention 108+, which aligned the Convention with the European Union's General Data Protection Regulation, the GDPR. And here's a fact to remember: the United States is not expected to ratify Convention 108 or 108+.

The fourth is the APEC Privacy Framework of 2004. APEC is the Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation, a group of 21 Pacific-rim members across Asia and the Americas. The crucial contrast for the exam: unlike the European Union, APEC operates under nonbinding agreement. The APEC Framework has nine principles that largely mirror the OECD, but it opens with a distinctive one — Preventing Harm — and it's more explicit about exceptions. Also remember: in 2022, several economies including the United States, Canada, Japan, Korea, the Philippines, Singapore, and Chinese Taipei announced the Global Cross-Border Privacy Rules Forum, which is independent of APEC and lets non-APEC members join.

And the fifth codification is the 2009 Madrid Resolution, formally the International Standards on the Protection of Personal Data and Privacy. Here's a subtle point the exam may probe: the Madrid Resolution was approved by the independent data protection and privacy commissioners — not by the governments themselves. Its principles include lawfulness and fairness, purpose specification, proportionality, data quality, openness, and accountability.

Quick recap: memorize the five FIPs codifications with dates — HEW 1973, OECD 1980 with its eight principles endorsed by the FTC, Convention 108 in 1981 which is binding, the nonbinding APEC Framework of 2004, and the Madrid Resolution of 2009 approved by commissioners, not governments.

Now let's turn to how information technology drove all of this. Mainframe computers emerged by the 1960s, and the unprecedented accumulation of personal data raised fears of surveillance — think George Orwell's 1949 novel "1984" and "Big Brother is watching you." In response, in 1970 the German state of Hesse passed the first known modern data protection law, motivated partly by memories of abuses under the Nazi regime. And in that same year, 1970, the United States passed its first national privacy law, the Fair Credit Reporting Act, the FCRA, which focused solely on consumer credit information. Those two 1970 facts are very testable: Hesse, Germany for the first modern data protection law; the FCRA for the first U.S. national privacy law.

Now let's turn to personal versus nonpersonal information, because the exam leans hard on definitions here. In the United States, we use "personal information" and "personally identifiable information," abbreviated PII, for information covered by privacy laws — anything that makes it possible to identify an individual, like a name, Social Security number, or passport number, and also identifiable details like street address, phone, and email. A subset is sensitive personal information — in the U.S., that commonly includes Social Security numbers, financial information, driver's license numbers, and health information, all of which get extra protection. Now the contrast: nonpersonal information is what remains after you strip out the identifying elements — also called deidentified or anonymized data — and privacy laws generally don't apply to it. Here's a distinction the exam loves: anonymized versus pseudonymized. Pseudonymized data replaces identifiers with something like a unique numerical code, which renders the data only temporarily nonpersonal because it can be reversed to reidentify the person. That reversibility matters in situations like a drug trial where a medicine turns out to have adverse side effects and you need to find the affected participants.

The line between personal and nonpersonal is not always clear, and the classic example is the IP address, the Internet Protocol address. Watch this carefully: the European Union generally treats IP addresses as personal data. In the United States, federal agencies under the Privacy Act do not consider IP addresses covered — but the FTC has said that in connection with health care breaches, IP addresses are personal information. So the same data element gets different treatment across regimes, and technology shifts the line further — static IP addresses and the newer IPv6 scheme tie devices more closely to identifiable people. Another testable pairing: HIPAA, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, applies only to certain "covered entities" and only to "protected health information" — it's not a blanket law.

Next, sources of personal information — and note the same fact can fall into more than one bucket. Public records are collected and maintained by a government entity and available to the public. Publicly available information is broadly accessible to many people — telephone books, newspapers, search engines. Nonpublic information is not generally available due to law or custom — medical records, financial information, adoption records, a company's customer or employee database. The key insight: a name and address might be a public record on a real estate deed, publicly available in a phone book, and nonpublic inside a patient file all at once — and restrictions depend on which source you got it from.

Now the processing vocabulary, first widely used in the EU, which you must know cold. Processing means basically anything you do with personal data — collecting, storing, using, disclosing, erasing, and so on. The data subject is the individual the information is about — the patient, employee, or customer. The data controller is the organization that decides how and why data is processed, and it bears most of the legal obligations. The data processor processes data on behalf of the controller, often a third-party outsourcer — and remember, under the HIPAA Privacy Rule, processors are called "business associates." A processor can hire subprocessors, but none of them may process data outside the controller's instructions.

Quick recap: PII identifies a person and pseudonymized data can be reversed while anonymized cannot; the EU treats IP addresses as personal while U.S. treatment varies; and the three roles are data subject, data controller who decides, and data processor who acts on the controller's behalf.

Finally, let's turn to sources of privacy protection and the four world models. Protection comes from four sources: markets, technology, law, and self-regulation and co-regulation. Under self-regulation, notice the three components the exam may test — legislation, meaning who defines the rules; enforcement, meaning who brings the action, possibly a data protection authority, a DPA; and adjudication, meaning who decides whether a rule was violated.

Now the four world models. First, the comprehensive model — the government sets requirements across the whole economy, public and private, overseen by a DPA. The European Union is the prime example. Countries adopt it for three reasons: to remedy past injustices, like Germany reacting to the Nazi and Stasi eras; to ensure consistency with EU law, since the GDPR limits transfers to countries lacking "adequate" protection; and to promote electronic commerce. Critics say it's costly, one-size-fits-all, and may slow innovation. Second, the sectoral model — the United States is the classic example — where laws target specific industries like credit, health, video rentals, and financial data. Supporters like the tailoring and lower burden; critics point to the lack of a single DPA and to gaps and overlaps. Remember a gap example: the HITECH Act of 2009 filled a gap by requiring breach notification from vendors of personal health records who weren't HIPAA covered entities. And an overlap example: a HIPAA covered entity can be enforced by the Department of Health and Human Services, HHS, or by the FTC under its unfair-and-deceptive-practices authority. Third, the co-regulatory and self-regulatory models. Here's the distinction in one breath: co-regulation, like Australia's approach, has industry codes backed by government law — the U.S. Children's Online Privacy Protection Act, COPPA, is an example where FTC-approved codes count as compliance. Self-regulation, more typical of the U.S., has industry codes with no general underlying data protection law — think the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard, PCI DSS, and seal programs like TrustArc. Fourth, the technology model — using technical measures like encryption so that administrative rules matter less.

To wrap up, here are your must-remember phrases. Privacy is "the right to be let alone," Warren and Brandeis, 1890. Four classes: information, bodily, territorial, communications. Five FIPs frameworks: HEW 1973, OECD 1980 with eight principles endorsed by the FTC, binding Convention 108 of 1981, nonbinding APEC 2004, and Madrid 2009 by commissioners not governments. Two firsts in 1970: Hesse, Germany's data protection law and America's Fair Credit Reporting Act. Pseudonymized data reverses; anonymized data does not. The EU calls IP addresses personal; the U.S. depends on the regime. Data subject, controller decides, processor acts — and a HIPAA processor is a "business associate." And the four world models: comprehensive like the EU, sectoral like the U.S., co-regulatory and self-regulatory, and technology-based. Nail those, and Chapter One is yours.
